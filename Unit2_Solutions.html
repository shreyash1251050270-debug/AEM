Question 01 5 Marks

Sketch the construction of a DC Motor.

Construction of a DC Motor

A DC motor converts electrical energy into mechanical energy. It works on the principle that a current-carrying conductor placed in a magnetic field experiences a force (Fleming's Left Hand Rule).

Main Parts

  1. Yoke (Frame) — Outermost cylindrical frame made of cast iron or steel. Provides mechanical support and acts as a return path for magnetic flux.
  2. Pole Core & Pole Shoe — Creates the magnetic field. Pole shoes spread the flux uniformly in the air gap.
  3. Field Winding — Copper coils wound around the pole core. When current flows, they produce the main magnetic field.
  4. Armature Core — Cylindrical core of laminated silicon steel. Houses the armature windings and rotates in the magnetic field.
  5. Armature Winding — Conductors placed in slots of the armature core. Types: Lap winding and Wave winding.
  6. Commutator — Cylindrical assembly of copper segments insulated from each other. Converts AC induced in the armature into DC output (acts as a mechanical rectifier).
  7. Brushes — Carbon or graphite blocks that press against the commutator. Carry current between external circuit and armature.
  8. Shaft & Bearings — Shaft transmits mechanical power. Bearings support the shaft and reduce friction.

Working of DC Shunt Motor

In a DC Shunt Motor, the field winding is connected in parallel (shunt) with the armature winding across the supply voltage.

Working Principle:

  1. When DC supply voltage V is applied, current flows through both the field winding (Ish) and the armature winding (Ia).
  2. The field winding current creates a stationary magnetic field (flux Φ).
  3. The armature conductors carry current Ia and are placed in this magnetic field.
  4. By Fleming's Left Hand Rule, a force acts on each conductor, producing torque.
  5. As the armature rotates, a back EMF (Eb) is generated that opposes the supply voltage.
  6. At steady state: V = Eb + IaRa
Key Equations — DC Shunt Motor Supply voltage: V = Eb + Ia × Ra Supply current: I = Ia + Ish Shunt field: Ish = V / Rsh Back EMF: Eb = (Φ × Z × N × P) / (60 × A) Torque: T = (Φ × Z × Ia × P) / (2π × A) Where: Φ = flux/pole, Z = total conductors, N = speed (RPM) P = poles, A = parallel paths, Ra = armature resistance

Fig 2.2 — DC Shunt Motor circuit diagram

Characteristics of DC Shunt Motor:

  • Speed: Approximately constant (slightly drops with load increase)
  • Torque: Proportional to armature current (T ∝ Ia)
  • Applications: Lathes, drilling machines, fans, blowers, centrifugal pumps

Question 03 5 Marks

Numerical: A DC motor connected to 450V supply takes 120A on full load. Ra = 0.15Ω, Rsh = 150Ω. Calculate back EMF.

Given Data

Supply voltage (V) = 450 V
Total current (I) = 120 A
Armature resistance (Ra) = 0.15 Ω
Field resistance (Rsh) = 150 Ω

Solution

Step 1: Calculate Shunt Field Current (Ish)

Formula Ish = V / Rsh Ish = 450 / 150 Ish = 3 A

Step 2: Calculate Armature Current (Ia)

Formula Ia = I − Ish Ia = 120 − 3 Ia = 117 A

Step 3: Calculate Back EMF (Eb)

Formula V = Eb + Ia × Ra Eb = V − Ia × Ra Eb = 450 − (117 × 0.15) Eb = 450 − 17.55 Eb = 432.45 V

Answer

The Back EMF at full load = 432.45 V

Question 05 5 Marks

What is a Transducer? What role does it play in a Robot Actuator System? Illustrate with an example.

Definition of Transducer

Definition A transducer is a device that converts one form of energy into another. In robotics, transducers typically convert a physical quantity (displacement, force, temperature, pressure) into a proportional electrical signal (voltage, current, resistance change) for processing by the controller.

Role in Robot Actuator System

In a robotic system, transducers serve as the sensory interface between the physical world and the electronic controller. Their role in the actuator system is:

  1. Feedback Sensing: Transducers measure the actual output (position, velocity, force) of the actuator and send this information back to the controller for closed-loop control.
  2. Error Detection: By comparing the desired output with the actual output (measured by the transducer), the controller computes the error signal to correct the actuator's behavior.
  3. Safety Monitoring: Transducers can detect excessive force, overheating, or over-speed conditions to trigger safety stops.
  4. Signal Conditioning: Some transducers convert non-electrical signals into electrical signals that the digital controller can process.

Fig 2.3 — Role of transducer in the actuator feedback loop

Example: Rotary Encoder in a Servo Motor System

A rotary encoder (optical transducer) is attached to the shaft of a servo motor:

  • The encoder converts mechanical rotation (angle) into digital electrical pulses.
  • The controller reads these pulses to determine the actual angular position of the motor shaft.
  • If the desired angle is 90° but the encoder reads 85°, the error signal (5°) drives the motor further.
  • This closed-loop operation ensures precise positioning — essential in robotic arm joint control.

Question 06 5 Marks

Discuss merits and demerits of Hydraulic Actuators used in Robotic Systems.

Definition Hydraulic actuators use pressurized fluid (oil) to generate linear or rotary motion. They operate on Pascal's Law: pressure applied to a confined fluid is transmitted equally in all directions.

Merits (Advantages)

  1. Very high force/torque output: Can produce extremely large forces with relatively small actuator size — ideal for heavy-duty industrial robots.
  2. Excellent power-to-weight ratio: Hydraulic motors are much lighter than electric motors of equivalent power.
  3. Smooth and precise control: Fluid flow can be precisely controlled using servo valves for accurate motion.
  4. High stiffness: Oil is nearly incompressible, providing rigid and stable positioning under heavy loads.
  5. Self-lubricating: The hydraulic fluid itself lubricates all moving parts, reducing wear.
  6. Overload protection: Pressure relief valves automatically protect the system from damage due to excessive load.
  7. Can hold loads without energy: Check valves can lock the actuator in position without continuous energy supply.

Demerits (Disadvantages)

  1. Oil leakage: Hydraulic seals can develop leaks, causing environmental contamination and mess.
  2. High maintenance cost: Requires regular fluid changes, filter replacements, and seal inspections.
  3. Bulky system: Needs reservoir, pump, valves, filters — large footprint.
  4. Fire hazard: Hydraulic oils are flammable; leaks near hot surfaces can cause fire.
  5. Noise: Hydraulic pumps and valves generate moderate to high noise levels.
  6. Temperature sensitivity: Oil viscosity changes with temperature, affecting performance.
  7. Slow response time: Compared to electric actuators, hydraulic systems have slower dynamic response.
  8. Environmental concerns: Disposal of hydraulic fluid is an environmental issue.

Applications in Robotics:

Heavy-duty industrial robots, construction robots, excavators, car manufacturing (spot welding), and heavy material handling robots.

Question 07 5 Marks

Discuss merits and demerits of Pneumatic Actuators used in Robotic Systems.

Definition Pneumatic actuators use compressed air to produce linear or rotary motion. The air is compressed by a compressor, filtered, regulated, and directed to actuators through control valves.

Merits (Advantages)

  1. High speed of operation: Pneumatic actuators can achieve very fast motion — ideal for rapid pick-and-place operations.
  2. Clean operation: Air is the working medium — no contamination risk (ideal for food, pharmaceutical, and clean-room industries).
  3. Low cost: Pneumatic components are inexpensive compared to hydraulic and high-end electric systems.
  4. Simple design: Fewer components and simpler construction than hydraulic systems.
  5. Safe operation: No risk of fire or electrical shock. Compressed air is non-flammable.
  6. Works in extreme temperatures: Pneumatic systems can function in very hot or cold environments.
  7. Lightweight: Pneumatic actuators are generally lighter than hydraulic equivalents.
  8. Overload safe: Pneumatic actuators simply stall under overload without damage.

Demerits (Disadvantages)

  1. Low force output: Compressed air has low power density — cannot produce forces as high as hydraulic actuators.
  2. Air compressibility: Air is compressible, making precise speed and position control very difficult.
  3. Air leakage: Leaks at joints and fittings reduce efficiency and cause noise.
  4. Moisture contamination: Atmospheric air contains moisture that can cause corrosion in actuators — requires air dryers.
  5. Noisy operation: Air exhaust creates significant noise (requires mufflers/silencers).
  6. Limited stroke length: Practical limitations on cylinder bore size and stroke.
  7. Poor position control: Due to air compressibility, intermediate positioning is difficult without additional mechanisms.

Applications in Robotics:

Light-duty pick-and-place robots, packaging robots, pneumatic grippers, assembly line automation, food processing robots.

Question 08 5 Marks

Discuss merits and demerits of Stepper Motors used in Robotic Systems.

Definition A stepper motor is a brushless DC electric motor that divides a full rotation into a number of equal discrete steps. The motor can be commanded to move and hold at any one of these steps without requiring feedback.

Merits (Advantages)

  1. Open-loop position control: Can achieve precise angular positioning without encoders or feedback — reduces system complexity.
  2. Discrete step movement: Each pulse moves the motor by a fixed angle (e.g., 1.8° per step = 200 steps/revolution).
  3. High holding torque: Can hold position firmly at rest without consuming extra power (detent torque).
  4. Excellent repeatability: No cumulative error — positional accuracy is purely a function of step angle.
  5. Simple digital interface: Responds directly to digital pulse signals — easy to interface with microcontrollers.
  6. Low cost: Cheaper than servo motors for low-to-medium performance applications.
  7. Long life: No brushes or commutator — fewer wearing parts.
  8. Works at low speeds: Provides good torque at low speeds without gearing.

Demerits (Disadvantages)

  1. Resonance issues: At certain speeds, stepper motors can vibrate or resonate, causing loss of steps.
  2. Step loss at high speeds: If the load exceeds the motor's torque at high speeds, steps are missed — no feedback means no error correction.
  3. Torque decreases with speed: Torque drops significantly as speed increases.
  4. Noisy operation: Stepping action produces audible noise and vibration.
  5. Limited speed range: Not suitable for very high-speed applications.
  6. Energy inefficient: Draws full current even when idle (holding torque), generating heat.
  7. No feedback (open-loop): Cannot detect or correct errors — if a step is missed, the error accumulates.

Applications in Robotics:

3D printers, CNC machines, camera positioning systems, robotic arms (low-cost), automated stages, plotters.

Question 09 5 Marks

Discuss merits and demerits of Electric Actuators used in Robotic Systems.

Definition Electric actuators convert electrical energy into mechanical motion (linear or rotary) using electric motors (DC, AC, stepper, servo, BLDC). They are the most commonly used actuators in modern robotics.

Merits (Advantages)

  1. Highest precision and accuracy: With encoders and servo control, electric actuators achieve the finest positioning accuracy (±0.01 mm).
  2. Clean and quiet operation: No oil or compressed air — suitable for clean rooms, labs, and medical applications.
  3. Easy speed and position control: Speed, position, and torque can be precisely controlled using PWM, PID controllers.
  4. Compact and lightweight: Electric motors are compact with high power density (especially BLDC motors).
  5. Energy efficient: Convert electrical energy to mechanical energy with high efficiency (80–95%).
  6. Easy integration: Directly interface with digital controllers, microprocessors, and PLCs.
  7. Low maintenance: No fluid to change, no filters, no compressors needed.
  8. Programmable: Easily reprogrammed for different tasks and trajectories.

Demerits (Disadvantages)

  1. Lower force/torque compared to hydraulic: Cannot match the raw power output of hydraulic actuators for heavy loads.
  2. Overheating risk: Continuous operation under heavy load can cause motor overheating.
  3. Higher initial cost: High-performance servo motors and drives are expensive.
  4. Gear reduction often needed: Most electric motors run at high speed/low torque — gearboxes required, adding weight, backlash, and complexity.
  5. Dependent on electricity: Cannot operate during power failure without battery backup.
  6. Limited in hazardous environments: Sparking from brushes (in DC motors) can be dangerous in explosive atmospheres.

Applications in Robotics:

Almost all modern robots — industrial manipulators, collaborative robots (cobots), mobile robots, humanoids, surgical robots, drones.

Question 10 5 Marks

Discuss the merits and demerits of Servo Motors used in Robotic Systems.

Definition A servo motor is a rotary actuator that allows precise control of angular position, velocity, and acceleration. It consists of a DC/AC motor, a feedback sensor (encoder/potentiometer), and a control circuit that continuously adjusts the motor to match the desired output.

Merits (Advantages)

  1. Precise position control: Built-in feedback (encoder) enables accurate angular positioning — essential for robotic joints.
  2. Closed-loop operation: Continuously corrects errors — if the arm is pushed off position, the servo corrects it automatically.
  3. High torque at low speed: Can deliver high torque even at standstill and low speeds.
  4. Fast response time: Quick acceleration and deceleration — dynamic performance is excellent.
  5. Wide speed range: Maintains good performance across a wide range of speeds.
  6. Smooth operation: Provides smooth, vibration-free motion compared to stepper motors.
  7. Compact design: High power-to-size ratio with integrated electronics.
  8. Programmable: Speed, position, and torque profiles can be programmed digitally.

Demerits (Disadvantages)

  1. High cost: Servo motors with encoders and drivers are significantly more expensive than stepper motors.
  2. Complex control: Requires sophisticated control algorithms (PID tuning), driver electronics.
  3. Requires tuning: PID parameters need careful tuning for optimal performance — can oscillate or become unstable if poorly tuned.
  4. Gear backlash: Geared servos can have backlash, reducing effective precision.
  5. Overheating: Under heavy continuous load, servo motors can overheat.
  6. Not suitable for open-loop: Cannot operate without feedback — if the encoder fails, the system loses control.
  7. Limited rotation: Standard hobby servos are limited to 180° or 270° (though industrial servos can rotate continuously).

Applications in Robotics:

Industrial robot arm joints, CNC machines, RC vehicles, humanoid robots, robotic grippers, camera pan-tilt systems.

Question 11 5 Marks

Can a robotic system have multiple actuators? Can they be of different types? How?

Answer: Yes — Multiple and Mixed Actuators

A robotic system can absolutely have multiple actuators, and they can be of different types. In fact, most practical robots use multiple actuators of varying types to optimize performance for different functions.

Why Multiple Actuators?

  • Each degree of freedom (DOF) requires at least one actuator. A 6-DOF robot arm has a minimum of 6 actuators.
  • The end effector (gripper) requires its own actuator(s), separate from the arm joints.
  • Mobile robots need actuators for locomotion (wheels/legs) in addition to manipulation.

Why Different Types of Actuators?

  • Different joints and functions have different requirements — some need high force (hydraulic), some need speed (pneumatic), some need precision (electric servo).
  • Optimization: Using the right actuator type for each function improves overall performance, cost, and efficiency.

  • Electric servo motors for arm joint positioning (precision)
  • Small DC motors for instrument rotation
  • Pneumatic actuators for gripper jaws (quick, gentle gripping)

Question 12 5 Marks

Draw a block diagram for a typical Hydraulic Actuator system.

Hydraulic Actuator System

A hydraulic actuator system uses pressurized oil (fluid) to generate linear or rotary motion. It works on Pascal's Law: F = P × A.

Fig 2.5 — Block diagram of a typical Hydraulic Actuator system

Flow of Operation:

  1. Oil is stored in the reservoir
  2. The pump draws oil and pressurizes it
  3. Oil passes through the filter (removes contaminants)
  4. The pressure control valve maintains safe operating pressure
  5. The DCV directs oil to the correct port of the actuator
  6. The flow control valve regulates speed
  7. The actuator converts hydraulic pressure into mechanical motion
  8. Spent oil returns to the reservoir through the return line

Question 13 5 Marks

Draw a block diagram for a typical Pneumatic Actuator system.

Pneumatic Actuator System

A pneumatic system uses compressed air to produce motion. It is widely used in light-duty and high-speed robotic applications.

Fig 2.6 — Block diagram of a typical Pneumatic Actuator system

Flow of Operation:

  1. Air compressor draws atmospheric air and compresses it to 6–10 bar
  2. Compressed air is stored in the air receiver (acts as buffer)
  3. The FRL unit filters contaminants, regulates pressure, and adds lubrication
  4. The DCV (solenoid-operated) directs air to the correct port of the actuator
  5. The flow control valve regulates the speed of the actuator
  6. The actuator (cylinder/motor) converts air pressure into mechanical motion
  7. Spent air is released through the exhaust muffler to the atmosphere

Question 14 5 Marks

Which type of actuators will be more preferred in Robotic Systems for medical applications? Why?

Answer: Electric Actuators (specifically Servo Motors and BLDC Motors)

For medical robotic applications, electric actuators — particularly servo motors and brushless DC (BLDC) motors — are the most preferred choice. Here's why:

Reasons for Preferring Electric Actuators in Medical Robotics:

  1. Extremely High Precision: Surgical robots require sub-millimeter accuracy. Electric servo motors with high-resolution encoders can achieve positioning accuracy of ±0.01 mm — critical for procedures like microsurgery.
  2. Clean Operation: Medical environments require sterile, contamination-free operation. Electric actuators have no oil (unlike hydraulic) or compressed air exhaust (unlike pneumatic) — no risk of contaminating the surgical field.
  3. Quiet Operation: Operating rooms require minimal noise. Electric motors are the quietest actuator type — no pump noise, no air exhaust noise.
  4. Smooth and Controlled Motion: Jerky movements are unacceptable in surgery. Servo motors provide smooth, vibration-free motion with precise speed control.
  5. Compact Size: Medical robots must fit in constrained operating room spaces. BLDC and servo motors have excellent power-to-size ratio.
  6. Safety — Force Limiting: Electric actuators can easily implement torque/force limiting through current control, preventing injury to patients. This is called compliant control or impedance control.
  7. Easy Integration with Control Systems: Medical robots use sophisticated computer control with real-time feedback. Electric actuators interface seamlessly with digital controllers.
  8. No Fire Hazard: No flammable oils (as in hydraulic systems) — safe in the presence of surgical gases.

Why NOT Hydraulic or Pneumatic?

Actuator TypeWhy NOT suitable for medical robots
HydraulicOil leakage risk, contamination, bulky, noisy pump, fire hazard
PneumaticPoor precision (air compressibility), noisy exhaust, imprecise position control

Real-World Example: Da Vinci Surgical System

The Da Vinci Surgical Robot by Intuitive Surgical uses electric servo motors for all joint movements. It performs minimally invasive surgery with precision of <1 mm, demonstrating why electric actuators are the gold standard in medical robotics.
