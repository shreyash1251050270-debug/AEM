Question 01 5 Marks

Classify grippers and explain each type briefly.

What is a Gripper?

Definition A gripper is an end effector attached to the wrist of a robot arm, designed to grasp, hold, and release workpieces. It is analogous to the human hand.

Classification of Grippers

A. Based on Gripping Method (Primary Classification)

TypePrincipleExample
ImpactivePhysical contact — jaws/fingers exert gripping force by impact/clampingMechanical jaw grippers, parallel grippers
IngressivePenetration into the object surface (pins, needles, hooks)Needle grippers for textiles, pin-type for foam
AstrictiveAttractive force applied to the surface without penetrationVacuum (suction), magnetic, electrostatic grippers
ContigutiveDirect contact requiring bonding or adhesionChemical adhesion, thermal (freezing), surface tension grippers

B. Based on Actuation Method

  • Mechanical Grippers — Jaw/finger mechanism actuated by linkage, gears, or cams
  • Pneumatic Grippers — Compressed air drives the fingers (fast, light-duty)
  • Hydraulic Grippers — Oil pressure for heavy gripping forces
  • Electric Grippers — Motor-driven (precise force & position control)
  • Vacuum Grippers — Suction cups for flat/smooth surfaces
  • Magnetic Grippers — Electromagnets for ferrous materials

Question 02 5 Marks

Numerical: A gripper lifts a 4 kg object upward with acceleration 2 m/s². μ = 0.4, n = 2 fingers. Find gripping force per finger.

Given Data

Mass (m) = 4 kg
Acceleration (a) = 2 m/s² (upward)
Coefficient of friction (μ) = 0.4
Number of fingers (n) = 2
g = 9.81 m/s²

Solution

Step 1: Calculate the total force acting on the object

When the gripper lifts the object upward with acceleration, the total force the gripper must overcome is:

Total Force F_total = m × (g + a) F_total = 4 × (9.81 + 2) F_total = 4 × 11.81 F_total = 47.24 N

Step 2: Calculate the gripping force

The object is held by friction between the gripper fingers and the object. The friction force must equal the total force:

Friction Equilibrium Friction Force = Gripping Force × μ × n For equilibrium: F_total = F_grip × μ × n F_grip = F_total / (μ × n) F_grip = 47.24 / (0.4 × 2) F_grip = 47.24 / 0.8 F_grip = 59.05 N per finger

Answer

Gripping force per finger = 59.05 N

Question 03 5 Marks

Numerical: 10 kg workpiece, 3-finger gripper, μ = 0.3, FOS = 2. Calculate gripping force per finger.

Given Data

Mass (m) = 10 kg
Number of fingers (n) = 3
Coefficient of friction (μ) = 0.3
Factor of Safety (FOS) = 2
g = 9.81 m/s²

Solution

Step 1: Calculate the weight of the workpiece

Weight W = m × g W = 10 × 9.81 W = 98.1 N

Step 2: Apply Factor of Safety

Design Force F_design = W × FOS F_design = 98.1 × 2 F_design = 196.2 N

Step 3: Calculate the gripping force per finger

Gripping Force For equilibrium: F_design = F_grip × μ × n F_grip = F_design / (μ × n) F_grip = 196.2 / (0.3 × 3) F_grip = 196.2 / 0.9 F_grip = 218 N per finger

Answer

Gripping force per finger = 218 N

Question 04 5 Marks

Explain different types of mechanical grippers with neat sketches.

Mechanical Grippers

Mechanical grippers use physical contact (jaws/fingers) to grasp objects. They are the most commonly used grippers in industry.

1. Parallel Jaw Gripper

  • Two parallel fingers move linearly towards/away from each other
  • Maintains parallel grip throughout stroke — good for prismatic objects
  • Provides uniform grip force distribution

Fig 3.2 — Parallel Jaw Gripper

2. Angular / Pivoting Gripper

  • Fingers pivot about a fixed point (like scissors)
  • Wider opening range — can handle larger size variations
  • Grip force varies with finger position

Fig 3.3 — Angular (Pivoting) Gripper

3. Three-Finger (Self-Centering) Gripper

  • Three fingers arranged at 120° around a central axis
  • Self-centering — automatically centers cylindrical objects
  • Similar to a lathe chuck mechanism

4. Toggle / Linkage Gripper

  • Uses mechanical linkage mechanism to amplify gripping force
  • Toggle mechanism provides mechanical advantage at full closure
  • Used for heavy-duty gripping applications

Question 05 5 Marks

Explain pneumatic, hydraulic, and electric grippers in detail.

1. Pneumatic Grippers

Use compressed air to actuate the gripper jaws/fingers.

  • Working: Compressed air enters a cylinder, pushing a piston that drives the fingers through a linkage mechanism.
  • Advantages: Fast operation, clean, lightweight, simple design, low cost.
  • Disadvantages: Limited gripping force, difficult to control intermediate positions, noisy.
  • Applications: Pick-and-place, packaging, electronics assembly, food handling.

2. Hydraulic Grippers

Use pressurized oil/fluid to actuate the gripper jaws.

  • Working: Hydraulic fluid enters a cylinder, creating high pressure that moves the piston and drives the gripper fingers with great force.
  • Advantages: Very high gripping force, smooth motion, can hold heavy loads, self-lubricating.
  • Disadvantages: Bulky, oil leakage, contamination risk, higher maintenance, slower than pneumatic.
  • Applications: Heavy material handling, forging, die casting, automotive body panel handling.

3. Electric Grippers

Use an electric motor (DC, stepper, or servo) to actuate the gripper mechanism.

  • Working: An electric motor drives a lead screw or rack-and-pinion mechanism that opens/closes the fingers. Encoder feedback enables precise force and position control.
  • Advantages: Precise force and position control, programmable grip profiles, clean, quiet, energy efficient.
  • Disadvantages: Higher cost, slower than pneumatic, limited force compared to hydraulic.
  • Applications: Collaborative robots (cobots), electronics handling, laboratory automation, medical robotics.

Question 06 5 Marks

Explain factors considered in the design of grippers.

Designing a gripper requires careful consideration of multiple factors to ensure reliable and efficient operation:

1. Workpiece Characteristics

  • Shape: Regular (box, cylinder) vs irregular shapes — determines finger geometry.
  • Size & Weight: Determines gripper opening range and required gripping force.
  • Material: Fragile (glass, electronics) vs robust (metal) — affects gripping method and force limits.
  • Surface finish: Smooth, rough, oily, wet — affects friction coefficient and gripper pad selection.

2. Gripping Force Requirements

  • Must overcome gravity, acceleration forces, and dynamic loads during motion.
  • Factor of Safety (typically 2–3) must be applied.
  • Formula: Fgrip = (m × (g + a) × FOS) / (μ × n)

3. Type of Actuation

  • Pneumatic (fast, light duty), Hydraulic (heavy duty), Electric (precision), Vacuum (flat surfaces), Magnetic (ferrous metals).

4. Environmental Conditions

  • Temperature range, presence of dust/moisture, clean room requirements, explosive atmosphere.

5. Cycle Time & Speed

  • Opening/closing time affects overall production cycle time.

6. Number of Fingers

  • 2 fingers (most common), 3 fingers (self-centering), multi-finger (dexterous manipulation).

7. Compliance & Adaptability

  • Ability to handle slight misalignment between gripper and workpiece.
  • Soft/compliant grippers for delicate objects.

8. Weight & Size of Gripper

  • Must be lightweight to minimize payload on the robot arm.
  • Must fit within the workspace without collisions.

Open-Loop Control System

In open-loop control, the controller sends commands to the actuator without any feedback about the actual output. The system does not verify if the desired position/velocity was achieved.

Fig 3.4 — Open-loop control — no feedback

Closed-Loop Control System

In closed-loop control, the actual output is measured by sensors and fed back to the input for comparison. The error signal drives the controller to correct the actuator output.

Question 08 5 Marks

Explain performance criteria of control systems in detail.

The performance of a control system is evaluated based on several key criteria:

1. Stability

A system is stable if its output reaches and remains at the desired value (steady state) after a disturbance. An unstable system produces an output that grows unbounded or oscillates indefinitely. Stability is the most fundamental requirement.

2. Accuracy (Steady-State Error)

The steady-state error is the difference between the desired output and the actual output after the system has settled. A good control system should have zero or minimal steady-state error.

3. Speed of Response (Transient Response)

  • Rise Time (tr): Time for the output to rise from 10% to 90% of the final value.
  • Peak Time (tp): Time to reach the first peak (maximum overshoot).
  • Settling Time (ts): Time for the output to settle within a specified tolerance band (typically ±2% or ±5%) of the final value.

4. Overshoot

The maximum amount by which the output exceeds the desired value before settling. Expressed as a percentage of the final value. Less overshoot is desirable — excessive overshoot can damage the robot or workpiece.

5. Sensitivity

Measures how much the system's performance changes when system parameters (like gain or component values) vary. A robust system has low sensitivity to parameter changes.

6. Bandwidth

The range of frequencies over which the system can respond adequately. A higher bandwidth means the system can respond to faster-changing inputs.

7. Robustness

The ability of the control system to maintain acceptable performance despite uncertainties, disturbances, and parameter variations in the plant model.

Question 09 5 Marks

Explain factors required to consider in gripper selection.

Selecting the right gripper for a robotic application involves evaluating the following factors:

  1. Workpiece Properties: Shape (regular/irregular), size, weight, material (fragile/robust), surface condition (smooth/rough/oily), temperature.
  2. Required Gripping Force: Must be sufficient to hold the object during all phases of robot motion (including acceleration, deceleration). Use F = m(g+a)×FOS / (μ×n).
  3. Type of Gripping Action: External grip (clamping outside), internal grip (expanding inside a hole), encompassing grip (wrapping around).
  4. Number of Workpieces: Single piece or multiple pieces per cycle.
  5. Actuation Method: Pneumatic (speed), hydraulic (force), electric (precision), vacuum (flat surfaces), magnetic (ferrous).
  6. Cycle Time Requirements: How fast the gripper must open/close to meet production speed.
  7. Precision Requirements: How accurately the object must be positioned in the gripper.
  8. Environmental Conditions: Clean room, underwater, high temperature, explosive atmosphere.
  9. Weight of Gripper: The gripper is part of the robot's payload — lighter gripper means more payload capacity for the workpiece.
  10. Compliance/Flexibility: Ability to handle misalignment and variations in workpiece position.

Question 10 5 Marks

State advantages and limitations of Magnetic and Vacuum Grippers.

Magnetic Grippers

Advantages:

  1. Quick pick-up — instant activation (electromagnet) or no power needed (permanent magnet).
  2. Can handle objects with holes, uneven surfaces, or hot parts.
  3. No mechanical contact damage to the workpiece surface.
  4. Simple construction with no moving parts (permanent magnet type).
  5. Can pick up multiple small parts simultaneously.

Limitations:

  1. Only works with ferromagnetic materials (steel, iron, nickel) — cannot grip aluminium, plastic, wood, etc.
  2. Residual magnetism may cause small parts to stick even after release.
  3. Electromagnets generate heat during continuous operation.
  4. Part may retain unwanted magnetism after handling.
  5. Gripping force depends on surface area and air gap — irregular surfaces reduce force.

Vacuum Grippers

Advantages:

  1. Can handle a wide variety of materials — glass, plastic, metal, cardboard, wood.
  2. No damage to workpiece surface — gentle handling.
  3. Can handle flat, smooth, and even slightly curved surfaces.
  4. Simple and lightweight construction.
  5. Fast pick-up and release cycle.
  6. Can pick up large flat objects (sheets, panels) that jaws cannot grip.

Limitations:

  1. Requires a smooth, non-porous, clean surface — cannot grip rough, textured, or porous surfaces.
  2. Object must have a flat area large enough for the suction cup.
  3. Cannot handle very heavy objects (limited by vacuum pressure × cup area).
  4. Sensitive to surface contamination (dust, oil, moisture).
  5. Cannot grip objects with holes or perforations.
  6. Requires a continuous vacuum source (pump/venturi).

Question 11 5 Marks

What is coarse and precise gripping? List its applications.

Coarse Gripping (Power Grasp)

Definition Coarse gripping (also called power grasp) is a type of grip where the entire hand/gripper wraps around the object to provide maximum gripping force. The emphasis is on holding strength rather than fine manipulation.
  • Uses large contact area between fingers and object
  • Object is firmly locked in the gripper — minimal object movement possible
  • Low dexterity — cannot perform fine adjustments while holding
  • Human analogy: Holding a hammer handle or gripping a heavy bag

Applications:

  • Material handling and transportation of heavy parts
  • Loading/unloading machine tools (CNC lathes, press brakes)
  • Palletizing and depalletizing operations
  • Construction and demolition robots
  • Forging and casting part handling

Precise Gripping (Precision Grasp)

Definition Precise gripping (also called precision grasp) uses only the fingertips to hold the object with controlled, delicate force. The emphasis is on accuracy and dexterity rather than force.
  • Uses small contact area — fingertips only
  • Allows fine manipulation and reorientation of the object while held
  • Requires force sensors for controlled, gentle gripping
  • Human analogy: Holding a pen, threading a needle, picking up a small screw

Applications:

  • Electronics assembly (placing ICs on PCBs)
  • Surgical robotics (handling tissues, sutures)
  • Laboratory automation (handling test tubes, slides)
  • Watchmaking and jewelry assembly
  • Micro-assembly operations

Question 12 5 Marks

State method and applications of: (a) Impactive (b) Ingressive (c) Astrictive (d) Contigutive Grippers.

(a) Impactive Grippers

  • Method: Grips by physical clamping/squeezing using jaws or fingers that exert force through mechanical contact. The object is held by friction and mechanical interlocking between the fingers and the object surface.
  • Examples: Parallel jaw, angular jaw, three-finger chuck, toggle grippers.
  • Applications: Machine tending, pick-and-place, assembly, welding fixture holding, CNC machine loading.

(b) Ingressive Grippers

  • Method: Grips by penetrating into the object surface using pins, needles, or hooks. The gripping elements physically enter the material.
  • Examples: Needle grippers, pin-type grippers, hook arrays.
  • Applications: Textile and fabric handling (needles pierce fabric), foam material handling, carpet handling, agricultural harvesting (hooks for fruits).

(c) Astrictive Grippers

  • Method: Grips by applying an attractive force to the object surface without penetration. Uses vacuum, magnetism, or electrostatic attraction.
  • Types:
    • Vacuum (suction): Creates negative pressure to hold flat surfaces
    • Magnetic: Uses electromagnetic or permanent magnetic attraction (ferrous materials only)
    • Electrostatic: Uses electric charge to attract lightweight objects
  • Applications: Sheet metal handling, glass panel handling, PCB handling, paper/cardboard handling, automotive body panel assembly.

(d) Contigutive Grippers

  • Method: Grips by establishing a direct bonding or adhesion between the gripper and the object surface. Requires surface-to-surface contact and some form of bonding mechanism.
  • Types:
    • Chemical adhesion: Glue or adhesive applied to grip surface
    • Thermal (Freeze gripping): Water between gripper and object is frozen to create a bond
    • Surface tension: Liquid film between surfaces creates capillary adhesion
  • Applications: Handling of thin silicon wafers (freeze gripping), micro-part handling, fabric handling, food handling (surface tension).

Question 13 5 Marks

Numerical: Pneumatic gripper lifts 6 kg object, 2 fingers, μ = 0.5, piston diameter = 40 mm. Find air pressure required.

Given Data

Mass (m) = 6 kg
Number of fingers (n) = 2
Coefficient of friction (μ) = 0.5
Piston diameter (d) = 40 mm = 0.04 m
g = 9.81 m/s²

Solution

Step 1: Calculate the weight of the object

Weight W = m × g W = 6 × 9.81 W = 58.86 N

Step 2: Calculate the gripping force per finger

Gripping Force For equilibrium: W = F_grip × μ × n F_grip = W / (μ × n) F_grip = 58.86 / (0.5 × 2) F_grip = 58.86 / 1.0 F_grip = 58.86 N per finger

Step 3: Calculate the piston area

Piston Area A = π/4 × d² A = π/4 × (0.04)² A = π/4 × 0.0016 A = 0.7854 × 0.0016 A = 1.2566 × 10⁻³ m²

Step 4: Calculate the required air pressure

The pneumatic piston must produce the gripping force. Since the piston drives one finger:

Pressure F_grip = P × A P = F_grip / A P = 58.86 / (1.2566 × 10⁻³) P = 46,840 Pa ≈ 46.84 kPa ≈ 0.468 bar

Answer

Air pressure required = 46.84 kPa (≈ 0.468 bar or ≈ 6.8 psi)
