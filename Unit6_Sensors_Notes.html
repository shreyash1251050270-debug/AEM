Sensor vs. Transducer

A device that, when exposed to a physical phenomenon (temperature, displacement, force, etc.), produces a proportional output signal — electrical, mechanical, magnetic, etc.

A device that converts one form of energy/signal into another form. Generally used to describe an element that converts a change in some physical variable into an electrical signal change.

Signal Chain Diagram

All sensors are transducers, but not all transducers are sensors. A sensor senses a physical phenomenon; a transducer converts energy. The term "transducer" is broader.

Active vs. Passive Sensors

⚡ Active Sensors
  • Do not need external power source
  • Directly generate electric signal from environment
  • Self-powered by the phenomenon itself
  • Examples: Thermocouple, Photodiode, Piezoelectric sensor
🔌 Passive Sensors
  • Require external power — called excitation signal
  • Modify the excitation signal to produce output
  • More common in industrial settings
  • Examples: Strain gauge, Resistance Temperature Detectors

Sensors are classified based on their working principle and the parameter being measured. Here is the complete taxonomy:

Proximity / Range

Detects presence or absence of objects without contact. Subtypes: Inductive, Capacitive, IR, Ultrasonic.

👆

Measures AC or DC current using electromagnetic induction (CT) or Hall effect (IC-based).

Measures angular velocity. Used in drones, navigation, and stabilisation systems.

Converts rotary/linear position into digital pulses. Rotary & Linear types.

🧲

Detects magnetic field. Generates voltage proportional to field strength.

🌡️

Thermistor (NTC/PTC), RTD, Thermocouple, Semiconductor-based (LM35).

📳
Accelerometer

Measures acceleration, vibration, tilt. Used in smartphones, drones, industry.

Inductive Proximity Sensor

Working Principle: Electromagnetic induction. The sensor generates a magnetic field around itself. When a metallic object enters the vicinity, it changes the magnetic field — this change is detected electronically.

Inductive sensors can ONLY detect metallic objects. Non-metallic materials such as plastic, rubber, wood, water, and dust do not alter the magnetic field and therefore cannot be detected.

Inductive Sensor Working Principle

Advantages

  • More accurate than capacitive sensors
  • High switching rate
  • Can work in harsh environmental conditions

Disadvantages

  • Can only detect metallic objects
  • Operating range is limited

Capacitive Proximity Sensor

Working Principle: Works on the principle of capacitance between two surfaces. One surface is inside the sensor; the other is the target object itself, which creates an electric field. When the object enters the working zone, the capacitance changes — which is detected.

Key Advantage over Inductive: Capacitive sensors can detect any material — plastic, rubber, wood, dust, liquids — since these all change the capacitance. Only air cannot be detected.

NPN and PNP Type Sensors

Proximity sensors are available in two output transistor configurations that determine how current flows in the circuit:

NPN (Sinking)
  • Output transistor is NPN
  • Current flows into the output terminal (sinking)
  • Load is connected between sensor output and +VDC
  • Signal goes to ground (Switched Negative)
  • Common in Asian market devices
PNP (Sourcing)
  • Output transistor is PNP
  • Current flows out of the output terminal (sourcing)
  • Load is connected between sensor output and Ground
  • Signal goes to positive (Switched Positive)
  • Common in European market devices

NPN = Sinking (current sinks to ground through the switch). PNP = Sourcing (current is sourced from positive). Most PLCs specify which type they accept — mismatch causes no output!

Infrared (IR) Sensors

IR sensors use infrared light (invisible, wavelength 700nm – 1mm) to detect objects. They have a transmitter (IR LED) that emits IR rays, and a receiver (photodiode / phototransistor) that detects reflected or interrupted rays.

Three Types of IR Sensors

1. Intensity-Based (Reflective)

Easiest to implement. An IR LED emits light; a phototransistor detects how much reflects back. More current through the phototransistor = more light = closer object.

  • Applications: Object detection, line following, wall tracking, optical encoder (break-beam)
  • Limitation: Highly susceptible to ambient light; affected by object reflectivity and distance
  • Works best in controlled / indoor environments

2. Modulated IR (Proximity)

Uses a modulated (pulsed) IR signal. Receiver module includes built-in modulation decoder. Current through the IR LED must be limited using a series resistor.

  • Applications: Rough distance measurement, obstacle avoidance, wall/line following
  • Advantage: Insensitive to ambient light (decoder ignores unmodulated light)
  • Detection range varies with object reflectivity (white card detects farther than black)

3. IR Distance Sensor (Ranging)

Uses an IR emitter + focusing lens + position-sensitive detector (PSD). The spot position on the PSD directly corresponds to target distance — converts horizontal position to distance.

  • Distance range: 10 cm – 80 cm (typical)
  • Uses modulated IR — immune to ambient light
  • Impervious to object color and reflectivity
  • Applications: Accurate distance measurement, wall following

Tactile (Touch) Sensors

Tactile sensing includes any form of sensing that requires physical touching between the sensor and the object being sensed. Critical for robotic manipulation, pick-and-place, and assembly operations.

What Tactile Sensors Can Detect

  • Presence or absence of objects
  • Part shape, location, and orientation
  • Contact area, pressure, and pressure distribution
  • Force magnitude, location, and direction
  • Surface texture, joint checking, damage detection
  • Object classification: recognition and discrimination
  • Grasping: verification, slip detection, error compensation
  • Assembly monitoring and verification

Components of a Tactile Sensor System

  • Touch surface — interface with the physical world
  • Transduction medium — converts local forces into electrical signals
  • Structure — physical housing and support
  • Control / Interface — signal conditioning and communication

Tactile sensors are critical in robotics from picking delicate fruits (like oranges) to loading heavy machines. The most important application currently is locating, identifying, and organizing parts for assembly.

Contact Sensors (Switch Types)

Contact sensors work when a moving object physically touches electrical contacts, forcing them open or closed. Different mechanical actuator types serve different applications:

TypeDescriptionUse Case
Roller Type A roller wheel actuates the switch when pressed Light machinery, slow-moving parts
Hinge Lever A lever arm on a hinge actuates the switch Longer travel detection, doors
Plunger Type A vertical plunger depresses to actuate Precise position detection
Limit Switch Switch that stops motion at a defined limit CNC machines, elevators, conveyor stops
Micro Switch Very small switch with snap-action mechanism Precision applications, mice, appliances
Normally Open (NO)
  • Circuit is OPEN (off) in resting state
  • Circuit CLOSES when actuated
  • Current flows only when triggered
  • Safer default — less chance of accidental activation
Normally Closed (NC)
  • Circuit is CLOSED (on) in resting state
  • Circuit OPENS when actuated
  • Used for fail-safe applications
  • If wiring fails, the circuit opens safely

Current Sensor

Current sensors measure the flow of electrical current in a circuit. Two main methods are used:

Method 1: Electromagnetic Induction (Current Transformer — CT)

  • Based on Faraday's law of electromagnetic induction
  • An EMF is induced in the secondary coil based on the current in the primary
  • EMF is proportional to the primary current (EMF ∝ I_primary)
  • The turns ratio of the secondary coil is the crucial parameter
  • Current is calculated from the measured EMF
Relationship
EMF_secondary ∝ I_primary × (N₂/N₁)
N₁ = primary turns, N₂ = secondary turns

Method 2: IC-Based (ACS712 — Hall Effect)

The ACS712 is a popular IC-based current sensor module using the Hall effect. It provides electrical isolation and analog output proportional to current flow.

PinFunction
IP+ / IP–Current input terminals (in series with load)
VCCPower supply (5V)
GNDGround reference
OUTAnalog voltage output (proportional to current)
PWR LEDPower indicator

The ACS712 comes in 5A, 20A, and 30A variants. The output is 2.5V (zero current), and sensitivity is 66mV/A (30A version). Used extensively in 3-phase systems for monitoring all three phases simultaneously.

Ultrasonic Sensor

Sound Frequency Basics

Sound Frequency Spectrum

Working Principle

Ultrasonic sensors use sound waves above 20 kHz to measure distances. A transmitter emits ultrasonic pulses, which travel to the object, reflect, and return to the receiver. The time of flight is measured to calculate distance.

Distance Formula
D = (Time × Speed of Sound) / 2
Speed of sound ≈ 343 m/s (at room temperature). Divide by 2 because pulse travels to object AND back.

Rotary Encoders (Speed Sensors)

An encoder converts angular or linear position into digital pulses, enabling precise measurement of speed, direction, distance, and position. Used wherever motion parameters need to be monitored or controlled.

Internal Components

  • Coded disk — a disk with alternating opaque and transparent segments
  • LED light source assembly — emits light through the disk
  • Light sensor assembly (photodetector) — detects light/no-light
  • Sensing shaft — connected to the rotating object being measured

Working Principle

  1. Light from LED passes through the transparent segments of the coded disk
  2. As the shaft rotates, opaque lines interrupt the light beam
  3. The photodetector generates a pulse each time light is interrupted (OFF → ON transition)
  4. Pulses are counted by a counter/controller to determine speed, position, or direction
Encoder Output Pulse Signal

Types of Encoders

Rotary Encoder
  • Converts angular position to signal
  • Single or multiple tracks for higher resolution
  • More tracks → finer resolution and accuracy
  • Applications: motors, CNC, robots, printers
Linear Encoder
  • Converts linear position to signal
  • Used in plotters, pick-and-place machines
  • High precision length/position measurement
  • Applications: precision machining, imaging

Industrial Applications

  • Printers — paper feed position control
  • CNC Machines — axis position and speed
  • Robots — joint angle and end-effector position
  • Conveyor systems, packaging, sorting, filling
  • Cut-to-length applications and imaging systems

Temperature Sensors

Temperature sensors measure temperature via electrical signals generated by change in voltage or resistance. Critical in medical, HVAC, and industrial applications.

Type 1: Thermistor (NTC / PTC)

A thermally sensitive resistor that continuously changes resistance with temperature. Very small in size with glass or epoxy-coated sensing element.

NTC (Negative Temp. Coefficient)
  • Resistance decreases as temperature increases
  • Higher resistance at lower temperatures
  • Most common type
  • Used in temperature monitoring circuits
PTC (Positive Temp. Coefficient)
  • Resistance increases as temperature increases
  • Lower resistance at lower temperatures
  • Used in overcurrent protection
  • Self-resetting fuses

Type 2: RTD (Resistance Temperature Detector)

RTD resistance changes predictably with temperature. Consists of a film or precision wire wrapped around a ceramic or glass core.

  • Platinum RTD — most accurate, range: –200°C to 600°C, most expensive
  • Nickel RTD — less stable than platinum, lower cost
  • Copper RTD — lowest cost, least stable
  • Provides highly accurate linear output

Type 3: Thermocouple

Two wires of different metals bonded at two points. The varying voltage between the junctions reflects temperature changes.

  • Operating range: –200°C to 1750°C (widest of all sensor types)
  • Accuracy: 0.5°C to 5°C (lowest accuracy)
  • Non-linear — requires lookup table for conversion
  • Active sensor (self-generating, no external power for sensing element)

Type 4: Semiconductor-Based (e.g. LM35)

Incorporated into integrated circuits. Uses two identical diodes with temperature-sensitive voltage-current characteristics.

  • Range: –70°C to 150°C (narrowest range)
  • Output: linear response (10mV/°C for LM35)
  • Lowest accuracy of basic sensor types
  • Slowest response time
  • Cheapest and easiest to interface with microcontrollers

Optocoupler (Opto-Isolator)

An optocoupler transfers electrical signals between two electrically isolated circuits using light. Inside: an IR LED (emitter) and a phototransistor (detector). Prevents high voltage spikes from damaging low-voltage control circuits.

Optocoupler Internal Structure

Advantages

  • Compact, lightweight, and low cost
  • Works on AC or DC high-voltage signals
  • Very fast operation, low power consumption
  • Low noise and long lifetime (several decades)
  • High switching frequency (short ON/OFF times)
  • No contact bounce or arc interference
  • Excellent response at lower frequencies

Disadvantages

  • Cannot handle high current directly
  • Requires external biasing voltage
  • Poor high-frequency response
  • Phototransistor type has non-linear light-to-current relationship

Applications

  • Ground isolation between circuits
  • High voltage monitoring circuits
  • Lighting control and dimmer circuits
  • Sensing presence of physical objects (as a sensor)

Accelerometer Sensor

An accelerometer measures vibration or acceleration of motion of a structure. Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity with respect to time. Forces may be static (gravity) or dynamic (movement, vibration).

Working Principle — Seismic Mass on Spring

The basic underlying principle is like a damped mass on a spring:

  1. When acceleration is experienced, the internal seismic mass gets displaced
  2. The mass is displaced until the spring can move it at the same acceleration rate
  3. This displacement is used to calculate acceleration
  4. In piezoelectric types: mass squeezes piezoelectric material → electrical charge proportional to force → proportional to acceleration
Relationship
Charge ∝ Force = Mass × Acceleration
Since mass is constant: Charge ∝ Acceleration
Accelerometer Internal Structure (Simplified)

Advantages

  • Available in numerous designs: compression, shear, strain gauge types
  • Various mounting arrangements: stud, screw, glue-on
  • Available for high-temperature environments (up to 1,200°F)
  • Wide bandwidth — frequency and amplitude response
  • Robust construction with long-term reliability
  • Easy installation

Disadvantages

  • Requires contact with (mounting on) the measured object
  • Accelerometer mass must be <5% of vibrating component mass
  • Sensitive to mounting — must be secured firmly
  • Sensitive to cable noise and "whip" (capacitance change in dynamic bending)

Applications

  • Smartphones — orientation sensing, display auto-rotate, step counting
  • Drones — stabilize orientation during flight
  • Rotating Machinery — detect vibration and imbalance
  • Industrial Platforms — measure tilt and stability
  • Laptops — detect drop, protect hard disk by parking heads
  • Aerospace — aircraft, missiles, navigation systems
  • Seismology — earthquake detection (Quake-catcher networks)
  • Automotive — airbag deployment, traction control

Hall Effect Sensor

A Hall effect sensor detects magnetic fields. Based on the Hall Effect: when current flows through a conductor in the presence of a perpendicular magnetic field, a voltage (Hall voltage) is generated perpendicular to both the current and magnetic field.

Hall Effect Principle

Key Properties

  • Output varies in proportion to the strength and direction of the magnetic field
  • Non-contact — no physical wear, low maintenance
  • Robust design — immune to vibration, dust, and water (when sealed)
  • Solid-state device — very reliable, long life

Applications by Industry

IndustryApplication
Automotive Speed sensors, crankshaft/camshaft position, ABS (Anti-lock Braking System), seat position, airbag control
Consumer Electronics Smartphones and laptops (open/close detection), joysticks, gaming controllers
Industrial Automation Position sensing, speed detection, current sensing in machinery
Medical Devices Non-contact position sensing — high precision and reliability
Electric Motors Brushless DC motors — detect rotor position, feedback for motor control

The ACS712 is both a current sensor AND Hall effect sensor. It uses the Hall effect to measure current without breaking the circuit — completely non-contact. This makes it safe for both AC and DC current measurement.
