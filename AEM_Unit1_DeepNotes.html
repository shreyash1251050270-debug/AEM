Topic 01

Systems — Concept & Classification

What is a System?

The word system appears in everyday language — solar system, digestive system, operating system, transport system. What unifies them all? A system is an organized assembly or collection of components (physical or abstract) that are interconnected and work together to achieve a specific function or goal.

Every system has a recognizable boundary. What lies inside the boundary is the system; what lies outside is the environment. The system interacts with its environment by exchanging inputs and outputs across this boundary.

A system is an assembly of interconnected components with a defined boundary that receives inputs from the environment, processes them, and delivers outputs.

Topic 02

Control Systems

A control system is a mechanism that controls the input and the processing of a system in order to achieve a desired output (response).

Control systems are everywhere — from a simple immersion rod to the attitude control of a satellite. The key idea is: how accurately and reliably can we get the output we want from a given system?

Open Loop Control System

In an open-loop system, the output is not measured and not fed back to the input. The controller acts solely on the programmed input and has no knowledge of what the output actually turned out to be. It is also called a non-feedback system.

Figure 2.1 — Open loop control system
Pros

Simple design, inexpensive, easy to build and maintain.

Cons

Cannot correct for disturbances, environmental changes, or internal variations.

When used

When system is well-understood and disturbances are negligible.

Examples

Traffic light, washing machine timer, immersion rod, field-controlled DC motor, bread toaster.

Closed Loop Control System

A closed-loop system uses a feedback path to compare the actual output with the desired reference. The difference (the error signal) is used by the controller to adjust the system and drive the output toward the desired value. Because the control action is a function of the output, the system is self-correcting.

Figure 2.2 — Closed loop (feedback) control system
Error

e(t) = r(t) − c(t) — the difference between reference and actual output. The controller works to make this zero.

Pros

Self-correcting, accurate, handles disturbances and parameter variations automatically.

Cons

More complex, more expensive, can become unstable if not designed carefully.

Examples

Air conditioner, rocket launch system, radar tracking, human respiratory system, thermostat.

Topic 03

Electromechanical Systems (Mechatronics)

Origin

In 1969, a senior engineer at the Japanese company Yaskawa Electric first coined the word mechatronics — a portmanteau combining mechanics and electronics. Yaskawa registered it as a trademark in 1971. Today it refers to an entire interdisciplinary engineering field.

Mechatronics (electromechanical systems) is the synergetic integration of mechanical engineering with electronics and intelligent computer control in the designing, manufacturing, and production of products and processes.

Figure 3.1 — Mechatronics as intersection of disciplines

Subsystems of Mechatronics

1. Mechanical Systems

Classified as rigid, deformable, or fluid in nature. Mechanical subsystems can be hydraulic, pneumatic, rotational, or translational. They are interfaced with computers through sensors and actuators. Key components: control valves, gears, cylinders, chains, pistons, shafts, bearings, springs, cams.

2. Electrical Systems

Concerned with the behaviour of three fundamental quantities — charge (Q), current (I), and voltage (V). Electrical systems are integral to all electromechanical designs. Key components: generators, transformers, relays, circuit breakers, switches, motors.

3. Electronic Systems

Used to design control and signal-processing circuits:

  • Analog circuits — passive components (R, L, C) and active components (diodes, transistors, op-amps, ICs).
  • Digital circuits — combinational logic (gates, mux, decoders) and sequential logic (flip-flops, counters, shift registers).

4. Instrumentation & Control Systems

  • Covers transducers, signal conditioning elements, analog meters, display devices, recorders, printers.
  • Data acquisition systems (DAQ / data loggers) collect, process, and store data.
  • Control systems maintain or alter a quantity of interest (position, speed, temperature) in a desired manner.
  • Deals with time-domain and frequency-domain analysis, stability, Bode plots, Nyquist criterion.
  • Key components: stepper motors, synchros, servo motors, servomechanisms, actuators, PLCs, microcontrollers.
Figure 3.2 — General mechatronic system block diagram

Applications of Mechatronics

  • Automatic washing machines and dishwashers
  • CD players, VCRs, camcorders
  • Document scanners, MRI equipment
  • Integrated circuit (IC) manufacturing systems
  • Industrial welding robots
  • Fax machines, photocopiers, laser printers
  • Flexible manufacturing systems (FMS)
  • Air conditioners, elevator controls
  • Automotive systems (ABS, EFI, ADAS)
  • CNC machines, 3D printers

Topic 04

Robotics — Definitions & History

Origin of the Word "Robot"

The word Robot was first coined by Czech novelist Karel Čapek in his 1920 play Rossum's Universal Robots (RUR). In Czech, robot comes from the word robota, meaning forced labour or drudgery. Čapek depicted robots as humanoid machines that could replace human workers.

The term Robotics was first used in print by science fiction author Isaac Asimov in his 1942 short story "Runaround."

Definitions of Robot

Machines that can replace human beings as regards physical work and decision making are categorised as robots.

A machine that resembles a living creature in being capable of moving independently (as by walking or rolling on wheels) and performing complex actions (such as grasping and moving objects).

— Merriam-Webster Dictionary

A machine capable of carrying out a complex series of actions automatically, especially one programmable by a computer.

— Oxford English Dictionary

A robot is a re-programmable, multi-functional manipulator designed to move material, parts, tools, or specialised devices through variable programmed motions for the performance of a variety of tasks.

— Robotics Institute of America (RIA)

Definitions of Robotics

Robotics is the art, knowledge base, and know-how of designing, applying, and using robots in human endeavours.

Robotics is the science of designing and building robots suitable for real-life applications in automated manufacturing and other non-manufacturing environments.

Robotics draws from: physics, mathematics, mechanical engineering, electronics & electrical engineering, computer science, and cognitive science.

Topic 05

Asimov's Laws of Robotics

Isaac Asimov introduced these laws in his science fiction writings to address the ethical behaviour of robots. Though fictional in origin, they are widely cited in AI ethics and robotics design philosophy. The laws are prioritised — a higher-numbered law can only be followed if it does not violate a lower-numbered one.

Figure 5.1 — Hierarchy of Asimov's laws (higher priority = lower number)

Key insight for exams: The laws are arranged in strict priority order. A robot cannot follow Law 2 if doing so violates Law 1. It cannot follow Law 3 if doing so violates Law 2 or Law 1. The Zeroth Law, added later, supersedes all three original laws — a robot may harm an individual to save humanity at large.

Topic 07

Robot Anatomy & Terminology

Anatomy of a Robot Manipulator

The anatomy of a robot refers to the study of its skeletal structure — the physical construction of the manipulator. A robot manipulator has many analogies to the human arm.

Figure 7.1 — Robot manipulator anatomy
Base (Link 0)

The foundation of the robot. Usually floor-mounted or on a mobile platform. All subsequent joint-link numbering starts from here.

Links

Rigid structural members. Link-0 connects to Joint-1; Link-1 is the output of Joint-1 and input to Joint-2, and so on. Links do not deform under load (idealised as rigid bodies).

Joints

Connect adjacent links and provide controlled relative motion (rotation or translation). Each joint has an input link and output link.

Arm

The link-joint assembly that ensures mobility and reachability. Provides position of the end effector in space.

Wrist

Located at the end of the arm. Provides orientation (Roll-Pitch-Yaw) of the end effector. Usually has 3 DOF.

End Effector

Attached to the wrist. Performs the actual task — gripping, welding, painting, drilling. Analogous to the human hand.

Joint-Link Numbering Convention

The base is Link 0. The first joint is Joint 1. Link 0 is the input to Joint 1; the output of Joint 1 is Link 1. Link 1 is simultaneously the output of Joint 1 AND the input to Joint 2. This scheme continues: Link (n-1) → Joint n → Link n.

End Effectors

An end effector is attached to the wrist of the manipulator. It performs the required task. Two broad categories:

  • Grippers — mechanical (jaw/finger), vacuum/suction (cup), magnetic (permanent or electromagnet), electrical (servo-driven).
  • Tools — welding guns, spray paint guns, drilling spindles, screwdrivers, laser cutters, heating torches, ultrasonic probes.

Actuators

Electric motors

Most widely used. DC servo motors, AC servo motors, stepper motors. High precision, easy to control electronically.

Pneumatic

Compressed air. Fast response, lightweight, low cost. Suitable for small robots with low force requirements. Limited positional accuracy.

Hydraulic

Pressurised fluid. Very high force and torque. Used in large, heavy-duty robots. Slower response; oil leaks are a concern.

Topic 08

Robot Kinematics — Introduction

Kinematics is the science of motion. Robot kinematics applies geometry to study the movement of multi-degree-of-freedom kinematic chains — the links and joints that form the robot structure — to plan and control movement without regard to the forces causing it.

Kinematics analyses positions, velocities, and accelerations of links and joints. It provides the mathematical tools to answer: "Where does my robot end effector go?" and "What joint movements do I need?"

Kinematics vs Dynamics

KinematicsDynamics
Study of motion (position, velocity, acceleration)Study of motion and the forces causing it
Uses geometry and trigonometryUses Newton's laws, Lagrangian mechanics
Ignores mass, inertia, forcesIncludes mass, inertia, torque, gravity
Foundation for path planningFoundation for torque/force computation
Solved first (simpler)Solved after kinematics (more complex)

Reference Frames and Why They Matter

Coordinates of a point in 3D space are meaningless without specifying relative to what? A reference frame defines the origin and the directions of the X, Y, Z axes from which measurements are taken. In a robot:

  • World Frame (Frame 0) — fixed to the ground/base of the robot. All other measurements are ultimately referenced to this.
  • Joint Frame i — attached to Link i. Rotates and translates with the joint.
  • Tool Frame — attached to the end effector (tip of the gripper or tool).
  • Product/Object Frame — attached to the workpiece being manipulated.

Transforming between frames is the core mathematical operation in robotics. If L₁, θ₁ and L₂, θ₂ are known (Link lengths and joint angles), we can find the coordinates of the tool frame relative to the world frame.

The workspace (or work envelope) of a robot is the set of all positions that can be reached by the robot's end effector. Its shape depends entirely on the robot's configuration and the range of motion of each joint.

Topic 10

Degrees of Freedom (DOF)

The Degree of Freedom (DOF) of a robot is the number of independent motions it can perform — or equivalently, the number of independent variables required to fully describe its configuration in space.

Each independent motion is one DOF. A rigid body in free space has 6 DOF — 3 translational (along X, Y, Z) and 3 rotational (about X, Y, Z). When joints connect links, they add constraints that reduce the free DOF.

Figure 10.1 — 6 DOF of a rigid body in free 3D space

Why 6 DOF? To place an end effector at an arbitrary position (x, y, z) and with an arbitrary orientation (roll, pitch, yaw) in 3D space, you need at least 6 independent control variables — hence 6 joints minimum. A 7th joint provides redundancy: multiple solutions exist for the same end-effector pose.

Topic 11

Forward & Inverse Kinematics

Figure 11.1 — Forward kinematics (FK) vs Inverse kinematics (IK)

Forward Kinematics — In Depth

Given the link lengths (d₁, d₂, …, dₙ) and all joint angles (θ₁, θ₂, …, θₙ), compute the Cartesian coordinates (x, y, z) of the end effector in the world frame. This is a deterministic, unique-solution problem — one set of joint angles always gives exactly one end-effector position.

Method: multiply the transformation matrices for each joint in sequence:
T₀ⁿ = T₀¹ · T₁² · T₂³ · … · Tₙ₋₁ⁿ
The final matrix T₀ⁿ gives the position and orientation of the end effector (frame n) relative to the base (frame 0).

Inverse Kinematics — In Depth

Given the desired end-effector position (x, y, z) and orientation, find the joint angles (θ₁, θ₂, …, θₙ) required to achieve it. This is a harder problem because:

  • Multiple solutions may exist — the robot can often reach the same point with different postures (elbow up / elbow down).
  • No solution exists if the target is outside the workspace.
  • Redundant robots (DOF > 6) have infinitely many solutions — the extra DOF is used for obstacle avoidance.
  • Computationally expensive — often requires iterative numerical methods (Jacobian inversion, gradient descent).
Analogy
FK: Given road map and driving instructions → find where you ended up.
IK: Given where you want to go → find what road to drive and which turns to take.

Topic 12

Transformation Matrices

Coordinates of a Point in 3D Space

A point P in 3D space is described by its coordinates (x, y, z) relative to a reference frame. The distance from origin is d = √(x² + y² + z²). The projections on each axis are the dot products of the position vector with the unit vectors of each axis.

Translation of a Point

When a reference frame is translated (shifted linearly) relative to another, the coordinates of a point are recalculated by vector addition. If frame 2 is displaced from frame 1 by (a, b, c), then the coordinates of point P in frame 1 are:

Translation equations
P₁(x) = P₂(x) + a
P₁(y) = P₂(y) + b
P₁(z) = P₂(z) + c

In matrix form: P₁ = P₂ + d   where d = [a, b, c]ᵀ

Rotation of a Point

When frame 1 is rotated about an axis of frame 0, the coordinates in frame 0 are found by projecting each axis of frame 1 onto each axis of frame 0 (i.e., taking dot products of unit vectors). Since each axis is a unit vector of length 1, these projections are simply cosines and sines of the rotation angle.

Consider rotating frame 1 about the Z₀ axis by angle θ. X₁ and Y₁ rotate together while Z₁ stays aligned with Z₀.

Projection derivation (rotation about Z₀)
Projection of X₁ on X₀ = cos θ  ·  |X₁| = cos θ (since |X₁| = 1, unit vector)
Projection of X₁ on Y₀ = sin θ
Projection of X₁ on Z₀ = 0
Projection of Y₁ on X₀ = −sin θ
Projection of Y₁ on Y₀ = cos θ
Projection of Y₁ on Z₀ = 0
Projection of Z₁ on X₀ = 0, Y₀ = 0, Z₀ = 1

Why We Need the 4×4 Homogeneous Transformation Matrix

A pure 3×3 rotation matrix can only express rotation — not translation. But every real robot joint involves both. To combine rotation and translation into a single matrix operation, we use homogeneous coordinates by appending a 1 to the position vector, making it 4×1, and expanding the matrix to 4×4.

Denavit-Hartenberg (D-H) Convention

The Denavit-Hartenberg convention is a standard method of attaching coordinate frames to each joint of a serial robot and describing the transformation between consecutive frames using exactly 4 parameters:

D-H ParameterSymbolDescription
Link lengthaᵢDistance along Xᵢ from Zᵢ₋₁ to Zᵢ
Link twistαᵢAngle from Zᵢ₋₁ to Zᵢ about Xᵢ
Joint distancedᵢDistance along Zᵢ₋₁ from Xᵢ₋₁ to Xᵢ
Joint angleθᵢAngle from Xᵢ₋₁ to Xᵢ about Zᵢ₋₁ (the joint variable for revolute joints)

For each joint i, one D-H matrix Tᵢ₋₁ⁱ is computed. The total forward kinematic solution is the sequential product of all such matrices from base to tool frame.

Total FK Transform
T₀ⁿ = T₀¹ × T₁² × T₂³ × … × Tₙ₋₁ⁿ

Result: 4×4 matrix encoding position [x, y, z] and orientation [R] of end effector in base frame.

Exam Quick Reference:
• R_z: Identity in bottom-right; cos/sin in top-left 2×2; rest zeros
• R_x: Identity in top-left corner; cos/sin in bottom-right 2×2; rest zeros
• R_y: cos/sin entries in columns 1&3; middle diagonal = 1; note sign flip: R_y(θ) has +sinθ in position [1,3] and −sinθ in [3,1]
• 4×4 D-H matrix: top-left 3×3 = rotation; top-right 3×1 = translation; bottom row = [0 0 0 1]
• To go from joint angles → end-effector position: multiply D-H matrices sequentially (FK)
• To go from desired position → joint angles: solve inverse (IK) — may have multiple solutions
