Question 01 5 Marks

Define the following: (a) Sensor (b) Transducer.

(a) Sensor

Definition A sensor is a device that detects or measures a physical quantity (such as temperature, pressure, light, proximity, motion) from the environment and provides a corresponding output signal (usually electrical) that can be read by an observer or an instrument.
  • A sensor responds to a stimulus (physical input) and produces a measurable output.
  • Sensors are the "sense organs" of a robotic or automated system.
  • Examples: Thermocouple (temperature), LDR (light), Strain gauge (force), Proximity sensor (distance), Accelerometer (acceleration).

(b) Transducer

Definition A transducer is a device that converts one form of energy into another. In engineering, it specifically converts a physical quantity into a proportional electrical signal (or vice versa).
  • All sensors are transducers, but not all transducers are sensors.
  • A transducer can work in both directions:
    • Input Transducer (Sensor): Converts physical quantity → electrical signal. Example: Microphone (sound → voltage).
    • Output Transducer (Actuator): Converts electrical signal → physical quantity. Example: Speaker (voltage → sound), Motor (current → motion).

Sensor vs Transducer — Comparison

FeatureSensorTransducer
FunctionDetects/measures a physical quantityConverts one form of energy to another
DirectionTypically input device onlyCan be input or output device
OutputAlways produces electrical signalMay produce electrical or non-electrical output
RelationshipA sensor is a type of transducerA transducer is a broader term
ExampleThermistor, LDR, IR sensorPiezoelectric crystal (can sense and actuate)

Question 02 5 Marks

Explain the construction and working of any one: (a) Tactile (b) Ultrasonic (c) IR Sensor.

All three sensors are explained below. Answer any one in the exam.

(a) Tactile Sensor

Construction:

  • Consists of a flexible membrane on top, a spacer layer in the middle, and a conductive layer at the bottom.
  • The conductive layers are made of conductive ink or resistive material printed on flexible substrates.
  • When no pressure is applied, the spacer keeps the layers separated (high resistance).

Working:

  • When an object presses on the sensor, the flexible membrane deforms and makes contact with the bottom conductive layer through the spacer openings.
  • This decreases the resistance between the two conductive layers proportionally to the applied force.
  • The resistance change is measured as a voltage change using a voltage divider circuit.
  • Used to detect touch, pressure, force — gives robots a "sense of touch."

Applications: Robotic grippers (detect object contact), prosthetic hands, touchscreens, human-robot interaction safety.

(b) Ultrasonic Sensor (HC-SR04)

Construction:

  • Contains a transmitter (T) and a receiver (R) — both are piezoelectric transducers.
  • The transmitter converts electrical signals into 40 kHz ultrasonic sound waves.
  • The receiver converts reflected ultrasonic waves back into electrical signals.
  • Has 4 pins: VCC (5V), Trig (trigger input), Echo (output pulse), GND.

Working:

  1. A 10 μs HIGH pulse is sent to the Trigger pin.
  2. The sensor transmits 8 bursts of 40 kHz ultrasonic pulses.
  3. The pulses travel through air, hit an object, and reflect back.
  4. The receiver detects the echo. The Echo pin goes HIGH for a duration proportional to the round-trip time.
  5. Distance is calculated using:
Distance Formula Distance = (Time × Speed of Sound) / 2 Distance (cm) = (duration in μs × 0.034) / 2 Distance (cm) = duration / 58.2 Speed of sound ≈ 340 m/s = 0.034 cm/μs Divide by 2 because sound travels TO object and BACK.

Fig 6.1 — Ultrasonic sensor working principle (echo-based distance measurement)

Range: 2 cm to 400 cm. Applications: Obstacle avoidance robots, parking sensors, level measurement, security systems.

(c) IR Sensor (Infrared Sensor)

Construction:

  • Consists of an IR LED (emitter) and a photodiode or phototransistor (receiver/detector).
  • The IR LED emits infrared light (wavelength ~940 nm) invisible to the human eye.
  • The photodiode detects reflected IR light.
  • Often mounted on a module with a comparator IC (LM393) and a potentiometer for adjustable sensitivity.

Working:

  1. The IR LED continuously emits infrared light.
  2. If an object is present in front, the IR light reflects off the object and hits the photodiode.
  3. The photodiode's resistance decreases → voltage changes.
  4. The comparator IC compares this voltage with a threshold (set by potentiometer).
  5. If reflected light is detected → Output = LOW (object detected).
  6. If no object → Output = HIGH.

Range: 2–30 cm (adjustable). Applications: Line-following robots, obstacle detection, proximity detection, object counting, edge detection.

What is a Proximity Sensor?

Definition A proximity sensor detects the presence or absence of an object without physical contact. It emits an electromagnetic field or beam and detects changes when an object enters the sensing range.

Detailed Working: Inductive Proximity Sensor

Working Principle:

  1. An oscillator circuit inside the sensor generates a high-frequency alternating magnetic field at the sensing face (coil).
  2. When a metallic object enters this field, eddy currents are induced in the metal surface.
  3. These eddy currents cause energy loss in the oscillator circuit, reducing its amplitude.
  4. A trigger circuit detects when the oscillation amplitude drops below a threshold.
  5. The output switches (ON/OFF) indicating object presence.

Detailed: Thermocouple

Construction:

  • Two wires of different metals (e.g., Chromel and Alumel for Type K) are joined at one end to form a junction.
  • The joined end is the measuring (hot) junction — placed at the point where temperature is to be measured.
  • The free ends form the reference (cold) junction — kept at a known temperature (often 0°C or ambient with compensation).

Working Principle — Seebeck Effect:

  1. When two dissimilar metals are joined and the junction is heated, a voltage (EMF) is generated across the free ends.
  2. This voltage is proportional to the temperature difference between the hot and cold junctions.
  3. The EMF is typically in the range of microvolt to millivolt per degree.
  4. A voltmeter or signal conditioning circuit measures this EMF and converts it to a temperature reading.

Industrial furnace monitoring, engine exhaust temperature, boiler temperature control, HVAC systems, scientific research, food processing.

Question 06 5 Marks

Discuss advantages, disadvantages, and applications of Optocouplers.

What is an Optocoupler?

An optocoupler (also called opto-isolator) is a device that transfers electrical signals between two isolated circuits using light. It contains an LED (input) and a phototransistor/photodiode (output) in a single package, with no electrical connection between them.

Advantages

  1. Electrical Isolation: Provides complete galvanic isolation between input and output (typically 2,500V to 7,500V isolation). Protects sensitive circuits from high voltages.
  2. Noise Immunity: Since the coupling is optical, electrical noise and interference from the input side does not transfer to the output side.
  3. Signal Level Shifting: Can interface circuits operating at different voltage levels (e.g., 5V microcontroller to 220V AC relay).
  4. Safety: Prevents electric shock and equipment damage by isolating high-voltage circuits from low-voltage control circuits.
  5. Compact Size: Available in small DIP packages (e.g., 4N35, PC817).
  6. High Speed: Can switch at frequencies up to MHz range (for high-speed optocouplers).
  7. No Mechanical Parts: Solid-state device — no wear, long life.

Disadvantages

  1. LED Degradation: The internal LED degrades over time, reducing the Current Transfer Ratio (CTR).
  2. Temperature Sensitivity: Performance varies with temperature changes.
  3. Limited Bandwidth: Standard optocouplers are slower than direct electrical connections.
  4. Power Consumption: The LED requires current to operate (5–20 mA).
  5. Non-Linear CTR: The Current Transfer Ratio is not perfectly linear — not ideal for analog signal transmission.

Applications

  • Microcontroller-to-relay interface — Isolating Arduino from high-voltage relay circuits.
  • Switch-mode power supplies (SMPS) — Feedback isolation between primary (AC) and secondary (DC) sides.
  • Motor drive circuits — Isolating control logic from power stage.
  • Medical equipment — Patient safety isolation between instruments and mains power.
  • Industrial PLCs — Isolating field I/O from controller.
  • Digital communication — RS-232, RS-485 isolation.
  • AC mains sensing — Zero-cross detection with isolation.

Question 07 5 Marks

Explain the working principle of a Hall Effect Sensor with a neat diagram.

Hall Effect Principle

Definition The Hall Effect states that when a current-carrying conductor is placed in a perpendicular magnetic field, a voltage (called the Hall voltage) is developed across the conductor in a direction perpendicular to both the current and the magnetic field.

Working of a Hall Effect Sensor:

  1. A thin semiconductor plate (Hall element) carries a current (I) in one direction.
  2. When a magnetic field (B) is applied perpendicular to the plate, the charge carriers (electrons/holes) experience a Lorentz force.
  3. This force deflects the charge carriers to one side of the plate, creating a charge accumulation on one edge and a deficit on the other.
  4. This charge separation produces a potential difference (Hall Voltage, VH) across the plate.
  5. The Hall voltage is proportional to the magnetic field strength and the current.
Hall Voltage Formula V_H = (B × I × t) / (n × e × A) Simplified: V_H = K_H × B × I Where: B = Magnetic flux density (Tesla) I = Current through the Hall element t = Thickness of the Hall element K_H = Hall coefficient (material property)

Fig 6.5 — Hall Effect sensor — magnetic field deflects charge carriers creating Hall voltage

Applications

  • Speed measurement: Magnetic gear tooth counting (RPM sensor in engines).
  • Position detection: Detecting magnet position (brushless DC motor commutation).
  • Current sensing: Measuring current through a conductor by detecting its magnetic field (non-contact).
  • Proximity detection: Detecting the presence of a magnet.
  • Automotive: Ignition timing, wheel speed (ABS), throttle position.

Question 08 5 Marks

What is an Optocoupler? What are the advantages of an optocoupler?

Definition

Definition An optocoupler (or opto-isolator) is an electronic component that transfers electrical signals between two electrically isolated circuits using light as the coupling medium. It contains an infrared LED on the input side and a phototransistor (or photodiode) on the output side, sealed in a light-tight package.

Working Summary:

  • When current flows through the input LED, it emits infrared light.
  • This light falls on the phototransistor on the output side.
  • The phototransistor turns ON, allowing current to flow in the output circuit.
  • There is no electrical connection between input and output — only optical coupling.

Advantages of Optocoupler

  1. Complete Electrical Isolation: Provides galvanic isolation of 2,500–7,500 V between input and output circuits. Protects sensitive microcontrollers from high-voltage damage.
  2. Noise Rejection: Optical coupling eliminates electrical noise, ground loops, and electromagnetic interference (EMI) from transferring between circuits.
  3. Voltage Level Translation: Can interface different voltage domains (e.g., 3.3V to 24V, or 5V to 230V AC).
  4. Protection: Isolates the control circuit from surges, spikes, and transients in the power circuit.
  5. Compact Size: Available in small IC packages (DIP-4, DIP-6, SMD). Common ICs: PC817, 4N35, 6N137.
  6. Unidirectional Signal Transfer: Signal flows only from LED (input) to phototransistor (output) — prevents back-flow of power surges.
  7. No Moving Parts: Solid-state device with long operational life and high reliability.
  8. Fast Switching: High-speed optocouplers (like 6N137) can operate at up to 10 MHz.

Question 09 5 Marks

Explain the construction and working of an Optocoupler with a neat diagram.

Construction

An optocoupler consists of the following main parts enclosed in a light-tight package (usually DIP-4 or DIP-6 IC):

  1. Input Side — Infrared LED: When current flows through the LED (pins 1 and 2), it emits infrared light proportional to the input current.
  2. Optical Channel: A transparent medium (air gap or transparent resin) allows IR light to travel from the LED to the detector.
  3. Output Side — Phototransistor: Receives the IR light on its base region. When illuminated, it becomes conductive, allowing current to flow between collector and emitter (pins 3 and 4).
  4. Light-Tight Package: Opaque plastic or ceramic housing prevents external light from entering while keeping internal light paths intact.

Fig 6.6 — Internal construction of an optocoupler

Working

  1. Input Signal Applied: A current-limited signal is applied to the LED (pins 1 and 2). When sufficient forward current flows (~5–20 mA), the LED emits infrared light.
  2. Optical Coupling: The IR light crosses the isolation barrier (air gap or transparent resin) and strikes the base region of the phototransistor.
  3. Output Activation: The photons generate electron-hole pairs in the phototransistor's base, causing it to conduct. Current flows from collector (pin 4) to emitter (pin 3).
  4. Signal Transfer: The output current is proportional to the input LED current (governed by the Current Transfer Ratio, CTR — typically 50–200%).
  5. Isolation Maintained: Throughout this process, there is zero electrical connection between input and output — only light couples the signal.
Current Transfer Ratio (CTR) CTR = (I_output / I_input) × 100% Example: If CTR = 100%, I_LED = 10 mA Then I_collector = 10 mA

Question 10 5 Marks

What is an IR Sensor? Draw connection diagram of IR sensor with Arduino Uno.

What is an IR Sensor?

Definition An IR (Infrared) sensor is a proximity/object detection sensor that uses infrared light to detect objects. It consists of an IR LED (emitter) and a photodiode (receiver). When IR light reflected from an object hits the photodiode, the sensor detects the object's presence.

IR Sensor Module Pins:

  • VCC — 3.3V–5V power supply
  • GND — Ground
  • OUT — Digital output (LOW when object detected, HIGH when no object)

Connection Diagram: IR Sensor with Arduino Uno

Fig 6.7 — IR Sensor module connected to Arduino Uno

Arduino Code:

int irPin = 7;       // IR sensor OUT connected to D7

void setup() {
  pinMode(irPin, INPUT);
  Serial.begin(9600);
}

void loop() {
  int val = digitalRead(irPin);
  if (val == LOW) {
    Serial.println("Object Detected!");
  } else {
    Serial.println("No Object");
  }
  delay(200);
}

What is an Ultrasonic Sensor?

Definition An ultrasonic sensor measures distance by emitting 40 kHz ultrasonic sound pulses and measuring the time taken for the echo to return. The most common module is the HC-SR04.

HC-SR04 Specifications:

  • Operating Voltage: 5V DC
  • Range: 2 cm to 400 cm
  • Accuracy: ±3 mm
  • Trigger Input: 10 μs HIGH pulse
  • Echo Output: Pulse width proportional to distance

Arduino Code:

int trigPin = 9;
int echoPin = 10;

void setup() {
  pinMode(trigPin, OUTPUT);
  pinMode(echoPin, INPUT);
  Serial.begin(9600);
}

void loop() {
  // Send 10μs trigger pulse
  digitalWrite(trigPin, LOW);
  delayMicroseconds(2);
  digitalWrite(trigPin, HIGH);
  delayMicroseconds(10);
  digitalWrite(trigPin, LOW);

  // Read echo pulse duration
  long duration = pulseIn(echoPin, HIGH);

  // Calculate distance in cm
  float distance = duration * 0.034 / 2;

  Serial.print("Distance: ");
  Serial.print(distance);
  Serial.println(" cm");
  delay(500);
}

Question 12 5 Marks

With a suitable diagram, illustrate the construction and working of a Rotary Encoder.

What is a Rotary Encoder?

Definition A rotary encoder is an electromechanical sensor that converts the angular position or rotation of a shaft into digital electrical signals (pulses). It measures rotation direction, speed, and position.

Types

  • Incremental Encoder: Generates pulses as the shaft rotates. Measures relative change in position (direction + speed). Requires a reference point for absolute position.
  • Absolute Encoder: Provides a unique digital code for each angular position. Knows the exact position even after power-off.

Construction (Incremental Optical Encoder)

  1. Light Source (LED): An infrared LED emits light towards the disc.
  2. Code Disc: A disc with alternating opaque and transparent slots (or patterns) is attached to the rotating shaft. As the shaft rotates, the slots alternately block and pass light.
  3. Photodetector: A photodiode or phototransistor on the other side of the disc detects the light pattern.
  4. Two Channels (A and B): Two photo-sensors are placed 90° out of phase. This produces two square wave signals (Channel A and Channel B) that are shifted by 90° — this is called quadrature encoding.

Fig 6.9 — Rotary encoder construction with quadrature (Channel A & B) output waveforms

Working

  1. As the shaft rotates, the code disc passes light alternately through transparent and opaque sections.
  2. The photodetectors produce square wave pulses — the number of pulses indicates the angle of rotation.
  3. Two sensors (A and B) produce waveforms shifted by 90° (quadrature). By analyzing which signal leads, the direction of rotation is determined:
    • A leads B → Clockwise rotation
    • B leads A → Counter-clockwise rotation
  4. The frequency of pulses indicates the speed of rotation.
  5. Resolution is expressed as PPR (Pulses Per Revolution) — e.g., 360 PPR = 1° resolution.

Applications

  • Robotic joint position feedback — measuring servo motor shaft angle
  • CNC machines — tracking spindle and axis position
  • Automotive — steering angle, throttle position
  • User input devices — volume knobs, scroll wheels
  • Industrial conveyors — speed and length measurement
