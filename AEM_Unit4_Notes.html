PN Junction Diode — Fundamentals

The diode is the simplest non-linear circuit element. Like a resistor it has two terminals, but unlike a resistor it has a non-linear current-voltage (I-V) characteristic. Its most common application is in rectifiers that convert AC to DC.

Physical Structure

A PN junction diode is formed by joining a P-type semiconductor with an N-type semiconductor. The P-side is the Anode (+); the N-side is the Cathode (−).

PN Junction Cross-Section · Depletion region at interface

What is the Depletion Region?

At the junction, free electrons from the N-side diffuse to the P-side and recombine with holes. This leaves positively charged donor ions on the N-side and negatively charged acceptor ions on the P-side, forming a space-charge region that is depleted of mobile carriers. This region creates a built-in electric field opposing further diffusion.

Key Point
The built-in potential for silicon ≈ 0.6–0.7 V. For germanium ≈ 0.2–0.3 V. Current flows only when this barrier is overcome.

Biasing, V-I Characteristics & Specifications

The voltage applied to a diode is called bias voltage. There are two types.

Forward Bias

+ve terminal → P-type
−ve terminal → N-type

  • Opposes built-in field → depletion region narrows
  • At 0.6 V (Si) barrier disappears
  • Large current flows
Reverse Bias

+ve terminal → N-type
−ve terminal → P-type

  • Aids built-in field → depletion region widens
  • Only tiny leakage (reverse saturation) current flows
  • Breakdown at high reverse voltage

V-I Characteristics

Complete V-I Characteristic of PN Junction Diode
Three operating conditions
Zero Bias: No external voltage. Depletion region at equilibrium. No current.
Forward Bias: >0.6V applied (Si). Exponentially rising current. Low resistance.
Reverse Bias: Only tiny reverse saturation current (nA range) until breakdown.

Rectifier Circuits

A rectifier converts AC → DC. This process is called rectification. Three types are covered.

Half-Wave Rectifier

Uses a single diode. Only the positive half-cycle passes through; the negative half is blocked. Output is pulsating DC.

Formulas
Vdc = Vm / π ≈ 0.318 Vm
Vrms = Vm / 2
Efficiency ≈ 40.6%
Ripple factor = 1.21
Half-wave output

Full-Wave Bridge Rectifier

Uses 4 diodes in a bridge configuration. Both half-cycles are utilized — the diode pairs alternate. Output ripple frequency = 2× input frequency.

Formulas
Vdc = 2Vm / π ≈ 0.636 Vm
Vrms = Vm / √2
Efficiency ≈ 81.2%
Ripple factor = 0.48

04

Special Diodes

LED — Light Emitting Diode

A PN junction diode that emits light when forward biased. Electrons recombine with holes releasing photons.

  • Materials: Gallium Arsenide (GaAs), GaAsP, AlGaInP (red/orange/yellow), InGaN (green/blue)
  • Colour depends on material composition (bandgap energy)
  • Forward voltage: ~1.8–3.5 V depending on color
  • Does NOT work in reverse bias
Photodiode

Converts light → current. Operated in reverse bias.

  • Light creates electron-hole pairs in depletion region
  • Reverse current proportional to light intensity
  • Dark current: tiny current even without light
  • Applications: optical communication, sensors, solar cells

Zener Diode

A heavily doped PN junction designed to operate in the reverse breakdown region. Also called breakdown diode. It maintains a nearly constant voltage across its terminals when the reverse breakdown voltage (Zener voltage, Vz) is reached.

Zener Diode Symbol & V-I Characteristic
Zener Breakdown Mechanisms
Zener Effect: Occurs at low breakdown voltages (<5V). Heavy doping causes direct tunneling of electrons across narrow depletion region due to strong electric field.

Avalanche Breakdown: Occurs at higher voltages (>5V). Carriers gain enough energy to ionize atoms by collision, creating an avalanche multiplication of carriers.

Zener Diode as Voltage Regulator

Zener diode is connected in reverse bias in parallel with the load. As long as input voltage ≥ Vz, output stays constant at Vz. Series resistor Rs limits current. Used in power supply regulation circuits.

Regulation Formula
Vout = Vz (constant)
IR = (Vin − Vz) / Rs
IZ = IR − IL

Bipolar Junction Transistor (BJT)

A BJT is a three-terminal, current-controlled semiconductor device. Unlike a FET (voltage-controlled), BJT current gain is driven by base current. It uses both majority and minority carriers — hence "bipolar".

Types & Structure

NPN Transistor

N-type emitter | P-type base | N-type collector
Most common type. Current flows from collector to emitter. Conventional: IB into base, IE out of emitter.

PNP Transistor

P-type emitter | N-type base | P-type collector
Current flows from emitter to collector. IB flows out of base.

NPN BJT Physical Structure

Operating Regions

Biasing Table
REGION E-B JUNCTION C-B JUNCTION MODE
ActiveForwardReverseAmplifier
SaturationForwardForwardSwitch — ON (closed)
Cut-offReverseReverseSwitch — OFF (open)

BJT Working Principle (NPN)

The emitter-base junction is forward biased — electrons from the emitter (N+) are injected into the thin P-base. Only a small fraction recombine with holes in the base (forming IB). The majority (~95–99%) diffuse through to the reverse-biased collector junction and are swept into the collector forming IC.

Key Relations
IE = IB + IC (KCL at transistor)
IC = β × IB (current amplification)
IC = α × IE (CB current gain)
β = α / (1 − α) | α = β / (β + 1)

BJT Configurations & Characteristics

Common Base (CB)

Base is the common terminal to both input and output circuits.

  • Input: VEB and IE (emitter-base)
  • Output: VCB and IC (collector-base)
  • Current gain: α = IC/IE — always <1 (typically 0.95–0.99)
  • Very LOW input resistance (~22 Ω), VERY HIGH output resistance (~1 MΩ)
  • High voltage gain (~150), no phase inversion
  • Application: Pre-amplifier, high-frequency amplifier

Common Emitter (CE) — Most Widely Used

Emitter is the common terminal. This is the most commonly used configuration.

  • Input: VBE and IB
  • Output: VCE and IC
  • Current gain: β = IC/IB (25 to 300 typical)
  • Medium input R (~1 kΩ), High output R (~40 kΩ)
  • Highest power gain; 180° phase inversion
  • VCE = VCB + VBE
  • Application: General-purpose amplifier
CE Output Characteristics (IC vs VCE) for various IB

Common Collector (CC) — Emitter Follower

Collector is the common terminal. Input is applied between base-collector, output taken from emitter-collector.

  • Input: VBC and IB
  • Output: VEC and IE
  • Current gain: γ = IE/IB — very high (β + 1)
  • Very HIGH input R (~500 kΩ), very LOW output R (~50 Ω)
  • Voltage gain <1 (≈1), no phase inversion — acts as voltage follower
  • Application: Impedance matching, buffer stage

Example Problem
Given: β = 125, VCC = 5V, RC = 0.7 kΩ

BJT as Switch & Amplifier

BJT as Switch

A BJT toggles between two states using Cut-off (OFF) and Saturation (ON) regions.

Switch OFF — Cut-off Region

Both junctions reverse biased.
IB = 0, IC ≈ 0
VCE ≈ VCC (full voltage appears across transistor)
Transistor acts as open circuit

Switch ON — Saturation Region

Both junctions forward biased.
IB sufficient to drive into saturation
VCE ≈ 0.2V (VCE(sat))
Transistor acts as closed switch

Switching Conditions
For saturation (switch ON):
IB ≥ IC(sat) / β
IC(sat) = (VCC − VCE(sat)) / RC ≈ VCC / RC

For cut-off (switch OFF):
VBE < 0.6V → IB = 0 → IC = ICEO ≈ 0

BJT as Amplifier (CE Configuration)

In the active region, small changes in IB produce large changes in IC — this is amplification.

  • Small AC input signal superimposed on DC bias point (Q-point)
  • Output AC signal is amplified and inverted (180° phase shift in CE)
  • The Q-point (quiescent point) must be in the middle of the active region for maximum undistorted swing
Amplifier Gain
Voltage gain Av = −β × RC / Rin
Current gain Ai = β = IC / IB
Power gain Ap = Av × Ai

Field Effect Transistor (FET) & JFET

A FET controls current through a circuit via an applied voltage — it behaves like a voltage-controlled resistor. FETs are unipolar (conduction by one carrier type only), unlike BJTs which are bipolar.

FET Terminals

Gate (G)

Controls current flow — the "gatekeeper". Applies electric field to channel.

Source (S)

Source of charge carriers. Electrons enter here (N-channel) or holes (P-channel).

Drain (D)

Destination of carriers. Current exits at drain.

FET vs BJT Key Differences
PropertyBJTFET
ControlCurrent (IB)Voltage (VGS)
CarriersBoth (bipolar)One type (unipolar)
Input RLow kΩVery high MΩ–GΩ
NoiseHigherLower

JFET Architecture

An N-channel JFET has an N-type body (the channel) with P-type gate regions on both sides. The gate is reverse biased to create depletion regions that control channel width.

N-Channel JFET Cross-Section

JFET Operation

  • With VGS = 0: Channel fully open, maximum drain current flows (IDSS)
  • Increasing reverse bias on gate (more negative VGS for N-channel) → depletion regions grow → channel narrows → current decreases
  • At pinch-off voltage (VP): Channel is completely pinched, ID → 0

JFET Characteristic Curve Regions

Ohmic (Linear) Region

Low VDS. JFET behaves like a variable resistor controlled by VGS. Channel resistance = f(VGS).

Saturation (Pinch-off) Region

Higher VDS. Current is nearly constant for a given VGS. Acts as a voltage-controlled current source.

JFET Drain Current (Shockley's Equation)
ID = IDSS × (1 − VGS/VP)²

where IDSS = drain current with VGS=0
VP = pinch-off voltage

P-Channel JFET

Same as N-channel but with all polarities reversed. N-type gate, P-type channel. Gate must be forward-biased positive. Increasing positive gate bias increases channel resistance.

Advantages of JFET

  • Very high input resistance (gate draws almost no current) — ideal for sensitive measurements
  • Low noise at low frequencies
  • Radiation tolerant — reverse-biased junctions can handle significant radiation damage
  • JFETs are virtually obsolete in ICs but still used as discrete components

MOSFET — Enhancement & Depletion Types

MOSFET = Metal Oxide Semiconductor Field Effect Transistor. It is the foundation of modern integrated circuits — thousands to billions can be fabricated on a single chip. Used for switching and amplification in virtually all modern electronics.

MOSFET Structure

Unlike JFET, the gate in a MOSFET is electrically insulated from the channel by a thin layer of silicon dioxide (SiO2). This means the gate draws essentially zero current and the input impedance is extremely high (>10 MΩ).

MOSFET Types
Two main categories:
1. Enhancement MOSFET (E-MOSFET) — normally OFF, channel induced by gate voltage
2. Depletion MOSFET (D-MOSFET) — normally ON, channel exists without gate voltage

Each available in N-channel and P-channel variants.

Enhancement MOSFET (E-MOSFET)

The E-MOSFET operates only in enhancement mode. There is no pre-existing channel — the substrate extends completely to the SiO2 layer.

  • N-channel: requires positive VGS above threshold (VT) to create channel
  • Positive gate attracts electrons to surface of P-substrate, forming an inversion layer (N-channel)
  • Increasing VGS increases channel conductivity (more electrons induced)
  • For VGS < VT: no channel, transistor is OFF
  • For VGS > VT: channel formed, current flows — transistor is ON
N-Channel E-MOSFET Cross-Section

Depletion MOSFET (D-MOSFET)

Has a physically diffused channel connecting source and drain — so current flows even with VGS = 0. The gate can deplete or enhance this channel.

Depletion Mode (VGS negative)

Negative VGS repels electrons from channel → channel narrows → current decreases. Eventually reaches pinch-off.

Enhancement Mode (VGS positive)

Positive VGS attracts more electrons → channel widens → current increases beyond IDSS.

Key Difference: E-MOSFET vs D-MOSFET
E-MOSFET: No channel at VGS=0. Only enhancement. Normally OFF.
D-MOSFET: Pre-existing channel. Can operate in depletion OR enhancement. Normally ON.

MOSFET — OFF State vs ON State

OFF State

P-N⁻ junction is reverse biased. Off-state voltage appears across N⁻ region. No current flows.

ON State

P-N⁻ junction slightly reverse biased. Positive gate voltage induces conducting channel. Drain current flows through N⁻ and channel. On-resistance = sum of all resistance elements.

Power MOSFET Features

  • Voltage-driven device → simple drive circuit (just charge gate capacitance)
  • Majority carrier device → fast switching, high frequency (100s kHz)
  • Better thermal stability than BJT
  • On-resistance (RDS(on)) increases rapidly with rated blocking voltage
  • Usually used at voltages <500V, power <10kW
  • 1000V devices available but practical only at low power (<100W)
  • Part number selected by on-resistance, not just current rating

Body Diode of Power MOSFET

An inherent antiparallel diode exists between source and drain due to the P-N junction between P-body and N⁻ drift region. This diode conducts in the reverse direction. Often exploited in inverter circuits for freewheeling, but its slow reverse recovery can cause switching losses.

MOSFET Equivalent Circuit Capacitances

Key Parasitic Capacitances
CGS — large, fairly linear (input capacitance, most important)
CGD — small, highly nonlinear (Miller capacitance — affects switching)
CDS — intermediate, highly nonlinear

Switching time is determined by the rate at which gate driver charges/discharges CGS and CGD.

IGBT — Insulated Gate Bipolar Transistor

The IGBT is a hybrid device — a combination of MOSFET and BJT. It combines the easy voltage-driven gate of a MOSFET with the low on-state voltage drop of a BJT (bipolar minority carrier injection).

IGBT = MOSFET (gate drive) + BJT (output characteristics)

IGBT Terminals

Gate (G)

Voltage driven like MOSFET. Controls turn-on/off.

Collector (C)

Corresponds to drain in MOSFET / collector in BJT. Positive voltage applied.

Emitter (E)

Corresponds to source in MOSFET / emitter in BJT.

Properties of IGBT

  • Device of choice for 500 V to 1700 V+ applications, 1–1000 kW
  • Positive temperature coefficient at high current → easy to parallel and build modules
  • Forward voltage drop = diode + on-resistance: typically 2–4 V
  • Easy to drive — similar to MOSFET (voltage gate drive)
  • Slower than MOSFET, but faster than Darlington, GTO, SCR
  • Typical switching frequencies: 3–30 kHz
  • Advanced devices: HVIGBTs at 3300V; 600V devices reaching 150 kHz

Logic Gates using Transistors

Transistors function as fast electronic switches. When the base-emitter diode is driven into saturation, VCE ≈ 0 → logic LOW output. When in cut-off, VCE ≈ VCC → logic HIGH output.

Why use Transistor Logic?
  • Transistors are cheaper than ICs
  • Can operate with voltages as low as 1.5V (ICs need min 3V)
  • ICs are ultimately made of transistors on-chip (TTL logic uses BJTs)
  • Only 1–2 transistors needed per gate

NOT Gate (Inverter)

Uses a single NPN transistor (e.g. BC547). One of the most fundamental gates.

Exam Tip
NAND and NOR are universal gates — any logic function can be built using only NAND or only NOR gates. NAND = AND + NOT (series transistors + inversion). NOR = OR + NOT (parallel transistors + inversion).
