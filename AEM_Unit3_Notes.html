Section 01

Introduction to Grippers

Core Definition
A gripper is a mechanical or robotic device that acts as the "hand" of a robot arm, enabling it to grasp, hold, manipulate, and release objects. It is the most common type of end effector.

The easiest analogy is the human hand — just as a hand enables holding, tightening, handling, and releasing an object, a gripper performs the same function for an industrial robot.

Industrial robots use grippers as end effectors for picking up raw and finished work parts. A robot can perform good grasping only when it obtains proper gripper selection and design.

Gripper vs. End Effector

Critical Distinction
An end-effector is any device at the end of a robotic arm designed to interact with the environment. Its exact nature depends on the application.

A gripper is a type of end effector — but not all end effectors are grippers. Tools like welding torches, spray guns, and drills are end effectors, not grippers.

Section 02

Mode of Grasp

There are four main categories based on how gripping is applied to the workpiece:

01
No Gripping

The workpiece is held in a jig (specially designed purpose-built holder) and the robot performs an activity on it without physically gripping it.

Examples: Spot welding, flame cutting, drilling

02
Coarse Gripping

The robot holds the workpiece, but gripping does not need to be precise. Accuracy in positioning is not critical.

Examples: Handling castings, dipping, unloading furnaces, stacking boxes or sacks

03
Precise Gripping

The robot holds the workpiece requiring accurate positioning. Higher-level control is necessary.

Examples: Unloading and loading machine tools

04
Assembly

Robot must assemble parts requiring accurate positioning + sensory feedback to monitor and correct movements in real time.

Examples: Electronic component assembly, precision fitting

Internal vs. External Gripping

Internal Gripper
  • Fingers expand outward to grip from inside a hole or cavity
  • Used when external surfaces are unsuitable for gripping
  • Better for tubular or hollow components
  • Provides stable grip for internal bores
  • Less common; more specialized
External Gripper
  • Fingers close inward to grip external surfaces of an object
  • Most common configuration in industry
  • Suitable for solid blocks, cylinders, irregular shapes
  • Simpler mechanism and control
  • Wide range of jaw designs available
Section 03

Classification of Grippers

General Category (by Gripping Principle)

Impactive

Jaws or claws that physically grasp by direct impact upon the object. The most common type.

Ingressive

Pins, needles, or hackles that physically penetrate the surface of the object. Used for textile, carbon, and glass fiber handling.

Astrictive

Apply suction forces to the object's surface — via vacuum, magneto-adhesion, or electro-adhesion.

Contiguous

Requires direct contact for adhesion — glue, surface tension, or freezing. No mechanical clamping.

Section 04

Mechanical Grippers — Deep Dive

A mechanical gripper uses mechanical fingers actuated by a mechanism to grasp an object. It is used for general pick-and-place applications.

Actuation Methods

Pneumatic

Uses compressed air. Most common in industry. Fast actuation, simple, clean, but limited force precision.

Operating pressure: typically 4–7 bar

Hydraulic

Uses pressurized fluid. Delivers maximum gripping force. Best for heavy loads. Cylinder diameter kept minimal to reduce surface area.

Force acts directly on piston → jaw/finger

Electric

Uses an electric motor. Offers highest precision. Most modern: has microprocessors enabling variable grip force, position, and speed control.

Programmable; best for delicate tasks

Mechanism Sub-Types

01
Linkage Grippers

No cam, screw, or gear involved. Movement occurs only due to links attached to input and output. The mechanism must be perfectly designed so the input actuator's motion is transformed into gripping action at the output. Simplest and most reliable for repetitive motions.

02
Gear and Rack Grippers

Input motion from gear motion drives connecting links into motion, producing gripping action at the output link. The rack converts rotary motion of the gear to linear motion of the jaw.

03
Cam-Actuated Grippers

Reciprocating motion of the cam imparts motion to the follower, causing fingers to produce a grabbing action. A variety of cam profiles can be employed — constant velocity, circular arcs, harmonic curves, etc. Different profiles give different speed/force characteristics.

04
Screw-Driven Grippers

Operated by turning a screw, which gives motion to connecting links and thus gripping motion to the output. The screw motion can be controlled by an attached motor. Self-locking when power is off (a major safety advantage).

Flexible & Multiple-Jaw Grippers

Flexible grippers consist of several linkages on each finger and can be two or multiple jaw designs. They can conform to object shapes, making them suitable for irregular or delicate objects. Multiple jaw grippers hold objects from multiple contact points, improving stability.

Hydraulic Gripper Selection Checklist
When selecting a hydraulic gripper, consider: Part weight and size · Part material · Clearance around the part · Operating environment (corrosive, food/beverage) · Robot motion path

Section 05

Vacuum Grippers

Vacuum grippers use suction cups (vacuum cups) to grip objects. They operate by creating a pressure differential — the atmospheric pressure pushing the object against the cup exceeds the internal vacuum pressure, holding the object securely.

How Vacuum is Generated

The vacuum can be generated using a venturi effect or a dedicated vacuum pump. When the suction cup contacts a flat/smooth surface and air is evacuated, the object is held in place.

Advantages of Vacuum Grippers

  • Can handle fragile objects without damage (glass, electronics)
  • Works on large flat surfaces efficiently (sheet metal, panels)
  • Can pick up objects from a single surface — no need to grip from multiple sides
  • Very fast pick-and-place cycle times
  • No risk of marring or scratching delicate surfaces
  • Can handle non-ferrous materials that magnetic grippers cannot

Disadvantages of Vacuum Grippers

  • Requires a smooth, non-porous surface for effective suction
  • Cannot handle objects with holes or perforations
  • May fail on rough, dirty, or wet surfaces
  • The suction cup can wear out over time
  • Relies on consistent air/vacuum supply

Section 06

Magnetic Grippers

Used to grip ferrous materials (iron, steel). A magnetic gripper uses a magnetic head to attract ferrous materials such as steel plates. The magnetic head is constructed with a ferromagnetic core and conducting coils.

Working Principle

Magnetic grippers are easier to control, but require a source of DC power and an appropriate controller. When the part is to be released, the control unit reverses the polarity at a reduced power level before switching off the electromagnet. This cancels the residual magnetism in the workpiece, ensuring a positive release.

Force Equation (Maxwell's Equation)

F = (IN)² / [2μ₀(R_A + R_M)² · A_c]
Where:
IN = Number of amp-turns of coil
A_c = Area of contact of the object with the magnet
R_A = Reluctance of magnetic paths through air
R_M = Reluctance of magnetic paths through metal

Gripping Force Requirement

P ≥ [a + g] × m × FoS
Where:
a = Gripper acceleration (m/s²)
g = Gravitational constant (9.81 m/s²)
m = Mass of object (kg)
FoS = Factor of Safety
✓ Advantages
  • Pick-up times are very fast
  • Variations in part-size can be tolerated
  • Capable of handling metal parts with holes
  • Needs only one surface for gripping
  • Safe in hazardous environments — no spark risk
✗ Disadvantages
  • Residual magnetism in workpiece can cause problems in subsequent handling
  • Magnetic attraction can penetrate beyond top layer — may lift multiple parts from a stack
  • Only works on ferrous materials
  • Requires DC power supply
Section 07

Adhesive Grippers

An adhesive gripper is a robot end effector that grasps objects by literally sticking to them. The gripper uses a type of adhesive (two-sided tape, glue pads, or microfiber surfaces) applied to a rod, sphere, or solid object.

Key Characteristics

  • A major asset in manufacturing — the adhesive can remain sticky for a long time without maintenance
  • The adhesive cannot be disabled by the object itself (passive holding)
  • Used for lightweight, flat, or thin parts where mechanical gripping is impractical
  • Microfiber adhesive grippers mimic gecko feet — rely on Van der Waals forces

Applications

Adhesive grippers are used to handle: delicate electronic components, thin films and foils, small pharmaceutical parts, fragile substrates. JPL (Jet Propulsion Laboratory) has developed gecko-inspired adhesive grippers for space robotics applications.

Limitation
Adhesive grippers typically handle only one part per cycle before the adhesive must be "reset" or replaced. They are generally limited to lightweight, clean objects.
Section 08

Gripper Selection Criteria

Selecting the correct gripper is critical for reliable robot performance. The following factors must all be considered:

I
Surface Accessibility

The gripper must have the physical ability to reach the surface of the workpart. Obstructions, fixtures, or confined spaces can limit gripper geometry.

II
Part Size Variation

Changes in workpart size (e.g., during machining: material is removed) must be accounted for while providing accurate positioning. The gripper must hold the part even when size varies.

III
Delicate Surface Protection

The gripper must not create distortion or scratches on fragile workparts. Resilient pads or soft-contact fingers may be required.

IV
Contact Area & Stability

The gripper should hold the larger area of a workpart if it has various dimensions — this increases stability and control in positioning. Use resilient pads for more grasping contact.

V
Weight of Workpart

The gripper must be rated for the weight of the object plus dynamic forces during acceleration. Must grip at or near the centre of mass.

VI
Robot Arm Speed & Direction

Speed of arm movement and the connection between direction of movement and gripper position on the part must be considered. Fast acceleration demands higher grip force.

VII
Friction vs. Physical Constraint

Determine whether friction or physical constriction (form-fitting geometry) helps grip the part. Also consider the coefficient of friction μ between gripper and workpart.

VIII
Operating Environment

Consider: heat, temperature, humidity, moisture, dirt, chemicals. These affect material choice, sealing requirements, and maintenance intervals.

Section 09

Gripper Design Criteria

Design Criteria (Must-Have Properties)

Strong & Durable

Must withstand repeated gripping forces, dynamic loads, and environmental stresses without failure.

Lightweight

Light gripper = less payload consumed by the gripper itself, leaving more capacity for the workpiece.

Dimensionally Stable

Must maintain workpiece orientation under high gravitational forces during all robot motions.

Built-in Compliance

Must have some automatic alignment capability to accommodate positioning tolerances — passive compliance preferred.

Fast Acting

Gripper open/close time affects cycle time. Must actuate quickly to keep robot throughput high.

Maintainable

Wear parts must be easily replaceable. Design should allow quick service without major disassembly.

Design Rules (Step-by-Step Approach)

i
Study the Gripping Requirements

Analyze parts to be grasped and tasks to be performed. Express gripping requirements keeping in view types of material, shape, and properties.

ii
Determine Additional Requirements

Identify any special conditions: high temperature, abrasives, dirt, chemicals, explosive environments, etc.

iii
Determine Specific Solutions

Develop designs combining modular solutions. Don't design from scratch — use existing proven modules where possible.

iv
Consider Multi-Gripper Design

Consider a design with two or three grippers mounted together at the end of the arm — enables handling multiple parts or different operations per cycle.

v
Redesign the Part if Feasible

If the part is difficult to grip, consider whether the part itself can be redesigned to be more grippable. This is often more cost-effective than a complex custom gripper.

vi
Specify and Design the Final Gripper

With all requirements known, specify the final gripper design with detailed drawings, material selections, and tolerances.

Section 10

Gripper Force Analysis

Core Concept
The basic purpose of the gripper mechanism is to convert input power into the required motion and force to grasp and retain an object. Force analysis determines the minimum gripping force to prevent slippage.

Fundamental Concept

For a rectangular block to be lifted, the gripping surface applies a force F_G along one axis through the centre of gravity of the block. The force preventing slippage due to gravity depends on:

  1. The angle θ subtended by the gripping surface with the horizontal
  2. The coefficient of friction μ between the gripping surface and the load surface
Static Gripping Force
This force evaluated represents the static force — the minimum force that must be applied to a stationary object (load). It is the lower bound for design.
Fig. 5 — Static Force Analysis on a 2-Finger Gripper

Minimum Gripping Force Formula

F_G = m(g + a) / (2 × μ × nf)
Where:
F_G = Gripping force per finger (N)
m = Mass of object (kg)
g = Acceleration due to gravity = 9.81 m/s²
a = Robot acceleration (m/s²)
μ = Coefficient of friction between gripper and part
nf = Number of fingers

Dynamic Gripping Force

F_g = m × g + a × F_s
Example — Parallel gripper CGS2-64-1:
Specification = 295 N (at 6 bar)
Acting force per finger = 147.5 N
m = 1.475 kg, g = 9.81 m/s², a = 0.1
Recommended handling weight = 1.475 kg (friction-locked connection)

Force Analysis — Pneumatic Cylinder Gripper

For a pneumatic cylinder driving a gripper mechanism, the key variables are:

Worked Example
Q. A 5 kg rectangular block is gripped in a two-finger gripper and lifted vertically at an acceleration of 27.5 m/s². The coefficient of friction between the two surfaces is 0.48. Calculate the minimum force that would prevent slippage.

Solution:
F_G = m(g + a) / (2 × μ)
F_G = 5 × (9.81 + 27.5) / (2 × 0.48)
F_G = 5 × 37.31 / 0.96
F_G ≈ 194.3 N per finger

Pivot-Type Grippers — Moment Equilibrium

For simple pivot-type grips holding boxes: apply moments of forces on one arm and sum them to zero (ΣM = 0). The actuating force required:

F_act × L_act = F_grip × L_grip
Example: If F_act = 20 kgf and L_act/L_grip ratio = 4,
then F_piston = 80 kgf to close the gripper

The gripping force is the arithmetic sum of the individual forces produced by each gripping element.

Section 12

Robot Control Systems

Control systems for robots can be classified into two fundamental groups: open-loop and closed-loop.

Open-Loop Control System

Definition
In an open-loop system, NO feedback is used. The controller must independently determine what signal to send to the actuator. The controller never actually knows if the actuator did what it was supposed to do.
Fig. 6 — Open-Loop Control System Block Diagram
✓ Advantages — Open Loop
  • Simple in construction and design
  • Economic in nature
  • Easy to maintain
  • Generally stable
  • Convenient when output is difficult to measure
✗ Disadvantages — Open Loop
  • Not accurate
  • Unreliable — errors accumulate
  • Any change in output cannot be corrected automatically
  • Not suitable for precision tasks

EXAMPLES Washing machine timer, toaster, traffic light, stepper motor systems

Closed-Loop (Feedback) Control System

Definition
In a closed-loop system, the output of the process is constantly monitored by a sensor. The sensor samples the output and passes the information back to the controller. Because the controller knows what the system is actually doing, it can make any adjustments necessary to keep the output where it belongs.
Fig. 7 — Closed-Loop (Feedback) Control System
✓ Advantages — Closed Loop
  • More accurate even in presence of non-linearities
  • Any error is corrected due to feedback
  • Large bandwidth range
  • Facilitates automation; makes system stable
  • Sensitivity to noise is reduced
✗ Disadvantages — Closed Loop
  • More complex and expensive
  • Overall gain is reduced
  • May become unstable if poorly designed
  • Requires sensors and feedback hardware

Servomechanism (Position Control)

In a servomechanism, the job of the controller is to have the controlled variable track a moving set point — for example, moving a robot arm from one position to another. The "heart" of the control system is the controller — an analog or digital circuit that:

  1. Accepts data from sensors
  2. Makes a decision based on the error signal
  3. Sends appropriate commands to the actuator

The error signal (E) = Set Point (SP) − Actual Output. Based on E, the controller decides the magnitude and direction of the signal to the actuator.

Section 13

PID Control Actions

The relationship between the error signal and the controlled output is called the controller action. This action may be Proportional, Derivative, or Integral of the error.

Fig. 8 — PID Controller Structure

Controller Types & Their Actions

ControllerActionEffectUse Case
P (Proportional) Output ∝ Error · Kp Reduces error but may leave steady-state error Basic speed/position control
I (Integral) Output ∝ ∫Error · dt · Ki Eliminates steady-state error; can cause overshoot Eliminating offset errors
D (Derivative) Output ∝ (dError/dt) · Kd Anticipates future error; reduces overshoot and oscillation Damping oscillations
PI P + I combined No steady-state error, good tracking Most industrial processes
PD P + D combined Fast response with damping Robot joint control
PID All three combined Best overall performance — accurate, fast, stable Most demanding control tasks
Controller Action Summary
(1) The error is sent to the controller → controller generates output to operate the actuator.
(2) The relation between error and controlled output = controller action.
(3) This action may be: P (proportional to error), D (rate of change of error), I (integral of error).
(4) Accordingly, P, PI, PD, and PID combinations are used to manipulate control parameters.
Section 14

Performance Criteria of Control Systems

When a step input is applied to a control system, the transient response is characterized by several key parameters before reaching steady state.

Fig. 9 — Step Response of a Control System (Unit Step Input)

Performance Parameters Defined

Delay Time (T_d)

Time for the response to reach 50% of the desired output for the first time.

Rise Time (T_r)

Time for the response to rise from 10% to 90% of the desired output (for underdamped systems). Indicates speed of response.

Peak Time (T_p)

Time at which the peak overshoot is observed — the time at which the response first reaches its maximum value.

Peak Overshoot (M_p)

The maximum deviation of the response above the desired output. Expressed as a percentage: M_p (%) = [(C_max − C_ss) / C_ss] × 100

Settling Time (T_s)

Time at which the transient phase dies out and steady state is reached. Usually defined as when response stays within ±2% or ±5% of desired output.

Steady-State Error (SSE)

The constant difference between the actual response and the desired output in the steady state region. A perfect controller has SSE = 0.

Transient vs. Steady State

Transient Phase
  • Initial response after input applied
  • System output oscillates or overshoots
  • Involves delay time, rise time, peak time, settling time
  • Characterized by T_d, T_r, T_p, M_p, T_s
  • Ends when response "settles" within tolerance band
Steady State
  • Long-term behavior after transients die out
  • Output should equal (or be close to) set point
  • Characterized by Steady State Error (SSE)
  • SSE = SP − actual output in steady state
  • Integral action in controller eliminates SSE
Quick Exam Reference — Key Relationships
Faster rise time → larger overshoot (trade-off)
P control alone → always has SSE
I control → eliminates SSE but can cause instability
D control → reduces overshoot and oscillation
PID → best balance: fast response + minimal overshoot + zero SSE
