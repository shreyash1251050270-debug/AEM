00 Mechanical Structure of a Robotic Manipulator

A robotic manipulator is built from a set of rigid links connected by joints, terminating in an end-effector (gripper). Each structural element has a specific mechanical role:

ComponentRole
BaseFixed mounting point; carries full load of arm. May rotate (waist joint).
Arms / LinksRigid members transmitting motion between joints. Length determines workspace reach.
JointsRevolute (rotation) or prismatic (linear). Each joint = one degree of freedom (DoF).
WristFinal 3 DoF (yaw, pitch, roll) giving orientation to end-effector.
SliderLinear joint enabling extension/retraction along an axis.
GripperEnd-effector; grasps, lifts, or manipulates objects.

1.2 Gears

Gears transmit torque and modify speed between shafts. The fundamental relationship:

Gear Ratio
N₁ × T₁ = N₂ × T₂
Speed Ratio = T₁ / T₂ (inverse of tooth ratio)
Torque Ratio = T₂ / T₁ (torque increases as speed decreases)
Gear TypeShaft ArrangementKey PropertiesApplication
Spur GearParallel shaftsSimple, efficient, straight teeth; noise at high speedClocks, gearboxes, conveyor drives
Bevel GearPerpendicular (or angled) shaftsChanges direction of power; conical shapeDifferential gear in automobiles
Worm & Worm WheelNon-intersecting perpendicular shaftsVery high reduction ratio; self-locking; low efficiency (~30–50%)Lifts, steering boxes, conveyor drives
Exam Tip — Speed in Gear Trains

In a gear train, the gear with the fewest teeth runs fastest. Smaller gear → higher speed, lower torque. Larger gear → lower speed, higher torque. In a 3-spur gear train with gears of different tooth counts, they do not all run at the same speed.

02 Flow of Information & Energy

2.1 Definitions

Sensor

A device that understands a change in the surroundings and creates a response signal. It perceives the physical world.

  • Eye → senses light rays from surfaces.
  • Thermometer → mercury senses temperature of a body.

Transducer

Collects sensor findings and converts them proportionately into another suitable quantity (usually electrical). In some cases sensor and transducer are a single device.

  • Microphone → sound waves → electrical signal.
  • Loudspeaker → electrical signal → sound waves.

Controller

The brain of the system. Makes decisions based on signals received from the transducer and sends commands to the actuator. Examples: PLC, microcontroller, PID controller.

Actuator

Creates linear or rotational motion in response to commands from the controller. It is the output device that does the physical work.

2.2 Types of Actuators

1. Pneumatic (Air pressured) 2. Hydraulic (Oil / Water pressured) 3. Electric (Motors / Solenoids)

03 Hydraulic Actuators

Hydraulic actuators use pressurised fluid (oil) to create force and motion. They operate at pressures up to 700 bar.

3.1 Components of a Hydraulic System

ComponentFunction
ReservoirOil storage + conditioning. Additives maintain oil properties. Acts as heat sink.
PumpPressurises oil; sends it to actuator. Pressure determined by system design requirement.
Pressure Control Valve (PCV)Releases excess oil back to reservoir to maintain required actuator pressure.
Direction Control Valve (DCV)Directs flow to correct actuator port (extend / retract). Uses Lever + Spring: lever operates, spring resets.
Flow Control Valve (FCV)Controls flow rate to/from actuator → controls actuation speed.
ActuatorPiston-cylinder (linear) or vane/gear motor (rotary).

Double Acting, Single-Ended Cylinder

The most common hydraulic linear actuator. Fluid from pump enters the blind end to extend the rod, and the rod end to retract it. The rod only exits one side (single-ended). Force equation:

Force & Pressure
F = P × A
P = Pressure (Pa = N/m²), A = Piston Area (m²), F = Force (N)

Volume of oil required = A × Stroke Length

3.2 Merits & Demerits

✓ Merits
  • Rugged and robust
  • Best for very high force / power applications
  • Holds force & torque constant without continuous pump supply
  • Pump & motor can be located far from actuator — minimal power loss
  • System has highest power-to-weight ratio
  • Flexible, easy control; instant & smooth reversal of motion
✗ Demerits
  • Fluid leakage is a constant issue — efficiency drops
  • Fire hazard (flammable hydraulic fluid)
  • Requires many spare parts; complex maintenance
  • System slows down noticeably if leakage occurs
  • Valve operations more difficult vs pneumatic
  • Heavier in weight

04 Pneumatic Actuators

Pneumatic actuators use compressed air as the working medium. Operating pressure is typically 5–10 bar. Source is a compressor (open system).

Types

  • Linear (cylinder) — Single-acting (spring return) and double-acting cylinders. Contains: piston, seals, barrel, inlet/outlet ports. Air from compressor enters and drives piston forward/backward.
  • Rotary — Converts linear piston motion to rotary motion via rack-and-pinion or vane mechanism. Inlet and outlet for compressed air from compressor.

Air Compressor Working Principle

A piston compresses air on its downstroke; inlet valve closes. On upstroke, compressed air exits through outlet valve. Seals prevent leakage around piston and barrel. The compression cycle: Suction → Compression → Discharge.

4.1 Merits & Demerits

✓ Merits
  • Fast response / fast process
  • Usable in extreme temperatures
  • Safe from fire hazards (air is non-flammable)
  • Lighter in weight
  • Valve operations easy
  • Automatic lubrication possible
  • Clean — no fluid contamination risk
✗ Demerits
  • Limited stroke length
  • Loss of air pressure is always a problem
  • Moisture entry drops air pressure; causes corrosion
  • Air may be contaminated → downtime & maintenance
  • Low pressure system (vs hydraulic)
  • Noisy operation (exhaust noise)
  • Compressible medium → less precise force control

05 Electric Actuators

Electric actuators use electrical energy to produce motion. They are the most common actuators in modern systems due to their precision, controllability, and cleanliness.

✓ Merits
  • Highest precision and control
  • Very high overall efficiency
  • Can be networked and programmed (digital control)
  • Quieter than pneumatic & hydraulic
  • No fluid leaks → environmentally clean
  • Easy integration with computers and PLCs
✗ Demerits
  • Higher initial unit cost than pneumatic/hydraulic
  • Continuously running motor overheats → wear on mechanical parts
  • Requires separate DC power supply (for DC types)
  • More complex control electronics needed

Sub-types of Electric Actuators

Solenoid Coil Relay DC Motor (Brushed) BLDC Motor AC Motor Stepper Motor Servo Motor

5.1 Solenoid Coil

A solenoid is a coil of wire that, when carrying current, creates a magnetic field and attracts a ferromagnetic core (plunger) into the coil, producing linear motion. This is the attraction–repulsion principle.

  • When current flows → magnetic field created → core pulled in.
  • When current stops → spring returns core to original position.
  • Used in: solenoid valves (control fluid flow in hydraulic/pneumatic systems), relays, door locks.
Relay

A relay is an electrically operated switch. A small control current through a solenoid coil attracts an armature, which closes (or opens) a separate high-current circuit. Enables low-power circuits to control high-power loads with electrical isolation.

06 Revision of Electromagnetism

6.1 Origin of Magnetism

Magnetism originates from unpaired electron spins in atoms. Electrons spinning clockwise (CW) or counter-clockwise (CCW) create tiny magnetic moments. The number of unpaired electrons and their spin orientations — along with crystal structure — are responsible for the magnetic properties of a material.

6.2 Earth's Magnetism

The Earth behaves as a huge bar magnet. Its magnetic north pole is near the geographical south pole (and vice versa). Geographical axis and magnetic axis are not coincident — they are offset by about 11°. A compass needle aligns with magnetic field lines, not geographical north.

6.3 Properties of Magnetic Lines of Force

  1. Invisible, imaginary, roughly elliptical lines that never cross each other.
  2. Closed loops: travel from North pole → South pole externally and South → North internally through the magnet.
  3. Lines parallel and in the same direction → REPEL each other.
  4. Lines act like stretched rubber bands — always try to contract (minimum length).
  5. Lines prefer a path of minimum reluctance (analogous to minimum resistance in electric circuits).

6.4 Right Hand Thumb Rule

For a current-carrying conductor: Thumb = direction of current; curled fingers = direction of magnetic field lines around the conductor.

For a current-carrying coil: Curled fingers = direction of current in coil; thumb = direction of North pole (field exits thumb side).

Rules Summary — Match These!

Right Hand Thumb Rule → Direction of magnetic field due to current in a single conductor.
Right Hand Grip Rule → Direction of field lines (North pole) due to current in a coil.
Fleming's Left Hand Rule → Motoring action: Force on conductor in magnetic field (F, B, I).
Fleming's Right Hand Rule → Generating action: Direction of induced EMF.
Faraday's Laws → Electromagnetic induction.
Lenz's Law → Direction of induced voltage opposes cause.

6.6 B–H Curve (Hysteresis Loop)

The B–H curve (hysteresis loop) shows how flux density (B) in a ferromagnetic material changes with the applied magnetising force (H) during a complete magnetisation cycle.

PointMeaning
O → ASluggish (non-linear) start — domain walls begin to move slowly
A → BRapid linear rise — domains align quickly with H
B → CSaturation — all domains aligned, no further increase in B
C → D (OD)H reduced to zero; B = Retentivity (Br) = residual magnetism remaining
D → EH reversed; B = 0 at point E → Coercivity (Hc) = force needed to demagnetise
E → FNegative saturation reached in reverse direction
F → G → CReturn to positive saturation completing the loop
Exam Tip

Retentivity = residual flux density when H is removed. Coercivity = reverse H needed to reduce B to zero. Hard magnetic materials → high coercivity (good permanent magnets). Soft magnetic materials → low coercivity, small hysteresis loop (good for transformer cores — low core loss).

Every Rotating Machine Must Have

Stator (stationary) + Rotor (rotating) + Air Gap separating them + shaft, bearings, foundation. One winding produces flux (field winding) and another is where working EMF is induced (armature winding).

7.2 Working Principle (Motoring Action)

"When a current-carrying conductor is placed in a magnetic field, it experiences a mechanical force." — This is Fleming's Left Hand Rule.

  • Field poles create a main magnetic flux across the air gap (N → S).
  • Armature current in conductors creates an armature flux (circular around conductors).
  • The two fluxes combine: one side of the conductor gets dense flux (strong repulsion), the other gets weak flux. This creates an unbalanced force → torque (couple) making the armature rotate.
  • Under field pole N, current is a dot (⊙); under S, a cross (⊗).

Role of Commutator — Unidirectional Rotation

When a dot conductor passes from N-pole region to S-pole region (after 180° of rotation), it would normally reverse force direction. The commutator reverses current direction in that conductor at exactly that moment — so the conductor always carries current in the direction that keeps torque in the same rotational direction. This gives unidirectional continuous rotation.

Commutator
Number of segments = Number of armature slots (practically)
Each segment is insulated from adjacent segments

7.3 Back EMF (Counter EMF)

When the armature rotates, conductors cut the main flux in the air gap. By Faraday's Law, an EMF is induced in them. By Lenz's Law, this EMF opposes the supply voltage — hence called Back EMF (Eb) or Counter EMF.

Back EMF Equation
Eb = (φ × Z × N × P) / (60 × A)

φ = flux per pole (Wb)
Z = total number of armature conductors
N = speed in rpm
P = number of poles
A = number of parallel paths
(Wave winding: A = 2 | Lap winding: A = P)

Also: Eb = V – Ia × Ra
∴ Ia = (V – Eb) / Ra

Since Eb ∝ φ × N, back EMF acts as a speed-sensing signal. At start, N=0 → Eb=0 → Ia = V/Ra (very large — needs starter). As speed rises, Eb rises, Ia reduces to safe value.

7.4 Torque Equation

Torque Derivation
Mechanical power = Eb × Ia = Ta × ω
ω = 2πN/60

Ta = (1/2π) × (φ × Z × P × Ia) / A [N·m]

Simplified: Ta = 0.159 × Ia × (φ × Z × P / A)

∴ T ∝ φ × Ia
(All other terms are machine constants)

7.5 DC Shunt Motor

DC Shunt Motor — Practical Values
V = 230 V DC
Im = 10 A (total supply current; depends on HP)
Ish = 1 A (shunt field current) → Ia = Im – Ish = 9 A
Ra < 1.0 Ω | Rsh = 200+ Ω
Eb ≈ 227–228 V (small drop across Ra + brushes)

Characteristics of DC Shunt Motor

Shunt motor has constant field flux (Rsh = constant → Ish = constant → φ = constant).

  • Speed-Load: N ∝ (V – Ia·Ra). As load increases, Ia increases slightly, Eb drops slightly, speed drops slightly. Essentially approximately constant speed motor. N ∝ (V – IaRa) / φ
  • Torque-Current: T ∝ φ × Ia ∝ Ia (since φ = constant). Linear relation. T ∝ Ia.
  • Speed-Torque: As T increases (load increases), speed decreases slightly — nearly flat curve.
Motor TypeCharacteristicApplications
DC Shunt MotorApprox. constant speed; medium starting torque; adjustable speedLathes, centrifugal pumps, machine tools, blowers, fans

Types of Brushed DC Motors

  • Self-Excited: Field winding powered from armature terminals.
    • Shunt Excited — field winding in parallel with armature
    • Series Excited — field winding in series with armature (T ∝ Ia²; high starting torque)
    • Compound Excited — both shunt and series field windings (cumulative or differential)
  • Permanent Magnet (PM): No field winding; permanent magnets provide field flux.

7.6 Numericals — Important Formulae Sheet

All DC Shunt Motor Formulae

Ish = V / Rsh

Solved Problem Examples

Problem Type 1 — Find Back EMF

Q: DC shunt motor, V = 450 V, Im = 120 A, Ra = 0.15 Ω, Rsh = 150 Ω. Find Eb.
A: Ish = 450/150 = 3 A. Ia = 120 – 3 = 117 A. Eb = 450 – (117 × 0.15) = 450 – 17.55 = 432.45 V

Problem Type 2 — Find Speed and Torque

Q: 4-pole, 500 V, wave-wound, Z = 522, φ = 0.025 Wb, Ra = 0.4 Ω, Ia = 80 A. Find Eb, N, Ta.
A: Eb = 500 – (80 × 0.4) = 468 V. For wave winding A = 2.
N = (468 × 60 × 2)/(0.025 × 522 × 4) = 56160/52.2 = 1075.9 rpm.
Ta = 0.159 × 80 × (0.025 × 522 × 4/2) = 0.159 × 80 × 26.1 = 331.9 N·m

Problem Type 3 — Half Load Speed

Q: 120 V shunt motor, Ra = 0.2 Ω, Rsh = 60 Ω, full load Im = 40 A, N₁ = 1800 rpm. Find half-load speed.
A: Ish = 120/60 = 2 A. Full load: Ia₁ = 40–2 = 38 A. Eb₁ = 120 – (38×0.2) = 112.4 V.
Half load: Im₂ = ~21 A (approx half). Ia₂ = 21–2 = 19 A. Eb₂ = 120 – (19×0.2) = 116.2 V.
N₂/N₁ = Eb₂/Eb₁ (φ constant) = 116.2/112.4 → N₂ = 1800 × 116.2/112.4 ≈ 1860.85 rpm

08 Brushless DC (BLDC) Motors

8.1 Why BLDC? — Merits & Demerits of Brushed DC vs BLDC

BLDC motors were developed to overcome the problems caused by brush-commutator contact in conventional DC motors.

Brushed DC — Problems
  • Continuous friction at commutator → wear & tear of brushes & segments
  • Maintenance cost increases
  • Heat generates carbon particles at brush contacts
  • Frequent brush replacement
  • Voltage drop at brush contacts (efficiency loss)
  • Starter needed (high inrush current)
  • Noisy operation
  • Separate DC power supply required
BLDC — Advantages
  • No brushes → no wear, no carbon dust
  • Quiet operation
  • Low maintenance
  • High power-to-weight ratio
  • Good electronic / software control
  • Moderate & small capacity motors
  • No commutator voltage drop
  • Works on simple attraction–repulsion (N–S) principle — not Left Hand Rule

8.2 Construction

A BLDC motor is a synchronous DC motor with two main parts:

  • Stator — has wound pole windings (coils) that can be electronically switched. The stator carries the armature winding in a BLDC (unlike brushed DC where it's on rotor). Typically 3-phase, 6 poles.
  • Rotor — carries permanent magnets (N and S poles). No windings. Rotates because stator poles are switched to always attract or repel rotor poles.

8.3 Working Principle

When stator poles are excited, attraction takes place between opposite poles of stator and rotor → rotor rotates. When a rotor pole approaches the attracting stator pole, that stator pole is switched OFF and the next stator pole is excited. The rotor pole keeps chasing the stator pole but never catches it → continuous rotation.

Key Concept — Driver Circuit

The motor driver circuit changes stator polarity at the correct moment. To know the exact location of the rotor pole at any instant, a Hall Effect Sensor (or back EMF in sensorless type) is used. The driver circuit timing depends on rotor position.

8.4 Hall Effect Sensor

Hall Effect: When a current-carrying conductor is subjected to a magnetic field perpendicular to current flow, an EMF is produced at right angles to both current and field. This EMF is detected by the sensor.

  • Hall sensors are placed in stator to detect rotor magnet position.
  • Output: digital signal (HIGH/LOW) indicating proximity of N or S pole.
  • Common IC: A3144 — 3 terminals: VCC (+5V), GND, Digital Output with pull-up resistor (10 kΩ) and optimal capacitor (104 = 0.1 µF).
  • 3 sensors placed 120° apart for 3-phase BLDC; output gives 6 possible rotor positions per revolution cycle.

8.5 Driver Circuit for BLDC

The most common configuration: three pairs of power MOSFETs arranged as a 3-phase bridge (H-bridge structure). Each pair governs switching of one phase.

  • MOSFETs controlled by PWM (Pulse Width Modulation) — converts DC input to modulated driving voltage.
  • PWM allows start-up current limiting and precise speed/torque control.
  • At each step, two windings are energized (one "high", one "low") and the third floats.
  • A deglitch circuit (using logic gates with delay) prevents false triggering from glitches (sudden temporary faults) in sensor signals.

8.6 Sensorless BLDC Control

Uses Back EMF (generated by rotating rotor magnets cutting stator windings) to determine rotor position — no physical sensors needed.

  • Back EMF ∝ rotational speed. At zero speed, back EMF = 0 (starting issue).
  • Controlled by microcontroller, DSP, or dedicated driver IC.
  • More complex but lower cost (no Hall sensors). Used in high-volume applications like drones, fans.

8.7 Types of BLDC Motors

TypeDescriptionUse Case
In-runner (Inner Rotor)Rotor with permanent magnets at center; surrounded by stator coils. Conventional design. Lower inertia.RC cars, servo drives, robotics
Out-runner (Outer Rotor)Stator coils at center; rotor with permanent magnets surrounds stator. Higher inertia, more poles → smoother rotation, more torque at lower RPM.Drones, electric bicycles, direct drive fans

Phase Configurations

BLDC motors available as single-phase, 2-phase, or 3-phase. 3-phase BLDC is most common — smoother torque, better efficiency.

Applications of BLDC Motor

CNC Machines Robotics Position Control Electric Vehicles Washing Machines Hard Drives DVD Drives Ceiling Fans (new) Blowers Air Conditioners Refrigeration Drones

09 Stepper Motors

9.1 Definition & Working Principle

A stepper motor is a special electrical machine which rotates in discrete angular steps in response to a programmed sequence of input electrical pulses. Unlike regular motors, it does not rotate continuously — each pulse advances it by one precise step angle.

  • Working: magnetic interaction between rotor and stator causes rotor to align to minimum reluctance or attraction position.
  • Step angle: typically 1.8° (200 steps per revolution). Can range from <1° to 45°.
  • Speed = pulse frequency × step angle. Higher pulse rate → higher speed → at very high rates, runs nearly like a DC motor.
  • When no pulse → rotor remains locked in current position (holding torque).

9.2 Features

  • Digitally controlled — programmable for Direction, Speed, Number of steps.
  • Brushless — no wear & tear.
  • Open-loop control possible (no feedback sensor needed for basic positioning).
  • Load vs no-load → no difference in running current.
  • Rating given in torque (N·m), not horsepower (doesn't rotate continuously).
  • Low cost, accurate, decent holding torque, moderate speed.
  • Compatible with digital devices (computers, microcontrollers).

9.3 Classification

Based on Rotor Construction

TypeRotor MaterialKey FeatureDetent Torque?
Permanent Magnet (PM)Permanent magnet (2 or more poles)Simple; attracts to stator poles. Cylindrical or projected pole rotor.Yes
Variable Reluctance (VR)Soft iron (non-magnetized)Rotor tries to achieve minimum reluctance position. Stator wound for 3 phases, 6 poles; rotor has different number of teeth.No
Hybrid (HSM)Axial permanent magnet + toothed rotorCombines PM and VR principles. Smallest step angle. High holding, dynamic & detent torque. Smooth operation. Micro-stepping possible.Yes
Detent Torque

The torque that holds the rotor in position when stator windings are de-energized. Present in PM and Hybrid types (because permanent magnet rotor is attracted to stator poles even without current). Absent in VR type (no permanent magnet).

Based on Stator Coil Connection

  • Unipolar — Centre-tapped coil. One end (+VIN at centre). Only half the coil used at a time. Simpler drive circuit (only 2 semiconductors per phase). But magnetic field is half strength. More leads available as motor inputs.
  • Bipolar — Two leads per coil. Requires H-bridge for direction control (4 MOSFETs per phase). More complex drive but maximum torque from all copper used.

9.4 Variable Reluctance Stepper — Step Sequence

Stator poles are switched ON in sequence. Rotor teeth align to the energized stator pole (minimum reluctance position). When rotor approaches that pole, it's switched OFF and next pole energized → rotor advances one step.

9.5 Step Angle Calculation

Step Angle — Variable Reluctance
Step Angle = 360° / (Nr × P)

Nr = number of rotor teeth
P = number of phases (stator coil sets)

Example: Nr = 50, P = 4 → Step Angle = 360/(50×4) = 1.8°
Steps per revolution = 360/1.8 = 200 steps
Speed from Pulse Frequency
Speed (rpm) = (Pulse frequency × Step angle) / 6

Example: Step angle = 0.9°, f = 500 Hz
Steps per rev = 360/0.9 = 400
Rev per second = 500/400 = 1.25 rps
Speed = 1.25 × 60 = 75 RPM

9.6 Stepping Sequences

ModeDescriptionStep Size
Full Step — One Phase ONOne phase energized at a time. Minimum torque.Full step (e.g., 1.8°)
Full Step — Two Phase ONTwo adjacent phases energized simultaneously. Higher torque.Full step (same angle, rotor aligns midpoint)

Unipolar Control Circuit Logic (Full Step)

Stepper coils A, B, C, D switched using transistors (or MOSFETs). The sequence table uses binary 1/0 to activate coils. At each step, rotor aligns to active poles. The sequence determines rotation direction: reversing the sequence reverses rotation.

Typical 4-Phase Full Step Sequence (One Phase ON)

Step 1: A=1, B=0, C=0, D=0

Step 2: A=0, B=1, C=0, D=0

Step 3: A=0, B=0, C=1, D=0

Step 4: A=0, B=0, C=0, D=1 → then repeat

Hybrid Stepper Motor Advantages vs VR

  • Smaller step angle possible
  • Higher holding, dynamic, and detent torque values
  • Less tendency to resonate
  • High efficiency at lower stepping rates

Hybrid Stepper — Disadvantages

  • Performance affected by change in magnetic strength (temperature, demagnetisation)
  • Higher inertia and weight (due to rotor magnet)
  • More expensive than VR type

9.7 Stepper Motor Advantages & Disadvantages

✓ Advantages
  • Rugged construction
  • Can operate open-loop (no feedback sensor needed)
  • Low maintenance (brushless)
  • Less likely to stall or slip
  • Simple construction, reliable
  • Full torque at standstill
  • Low cost
  • Excellent start/stop/reverse response
✗ Disadvantages
  • Requires dedicated driver/control circuit
  • Uses more current than DC motors (even at standstill)
  • Torque output drops rapidly at higher speeds
  • Can miss steps under overload (open-loop)
  • Resonance at certain speeds (especially VR/PM types)

Applications of Stepper Motors

10 Servo Motors

10.1 What is a Servo Motor?

A servo motor is essentially a combination of a DC/AC motor + gear train + position sensor + feedback circuit + control circuit — all housed together in one unit.

Key distinguishing feature: It can rotate through specific degrees (position control) in addition to continuous rotation. This makes it ideal for precise position control.

10.2 Internal Components

ComponentRole
DC MotorHigh-speed, low-torque motor — provides the rotational input.
Gear TrainReduces speed dramatically (e.g., to 30–40 RPM); multiplies torque correspondingly. Multiple gear stages.
Potentiometer (Position Sensor)Connected to end of gear train; turns through same angle as last gear. Resistance varies with angle. Position = calculated from resistance.
Control Circuit (H-bridge + comparator)Compares commanded position (from PWM) with actual position (from pot). Drives motor until error = 0.
Power ConnectorMotor power (+5V or +6V typically for hobby servos).
Signal Wire (PWM)Orange/yellow: 50 Hz pulse; pulse width 1–2 ms → 0°–180° range.

PWM Control of Servo

PWM Signal
Frequency: 50 Hz (period = 20 ms)
Pulse width: 1.0 ms → 0° position
Pulse width: 1.5 ms → 90° (neutral)
Pulse width: 2.0 ms → 180° position

Wire colours: Brown/Black = GND, Red = +VCC, Orange/Yellow = PWM signal

10.3 Speed Control of Servo Motor

  • Armature voltage control: Voltage to motor armature from servo system controls speed. N ∝ (V – Ia·Ra) / φ
  • Field (flux) control: Voltage to field winding controls flux, thus speed. N ∝ Eb / φ — reducing φ increases speed.
