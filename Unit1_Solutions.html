Question 01 5 Marks

What is a robot? How are robots classified?

Definition of a Robot

Definition A robot is a reprogrammable, multifunctional manipulator designed to move materials, parts, tools, or specialized devices through variable programmed motions for the performance of a variety of tasks. — Robot Institute of America (RIA)

The word "Robot" was coined by Czech novelist Karel Čapek in his 1920 play "Rossum's Universal Robots (R.U.R.)". In Czech, "robota" means forced labor or servant. The term "Robotics" was introduced by Isaac Asimov in 1942.

Classification of Robots

Robots can be classified based on multiple criteria:

A. Based on Application

  • Industrial Robots — Used in manufacturing for welding, painting, assembly, pick-and-place, material handling. Example: FANUC, ABB, KUKA.
  • Service Robots — Perform tasks for humans outside manufacturing. Examples: vacuum cleaners (Roomba), hospital delivery robots.
  • Military/Defense Robots — Used in bomb disposal, surveillance, drone operations.
  • Medical Robots — Surgical assistance (Da Vinci system), rehabilitation robots.
  • Space/Exploration Robots — Planetary rovers (Curiosity, Perseverance), space station arms (Canadarm).

B. Based on Configuration / Geometry

C. Based on Control Method

  • Servo Controlled Robots — Use feedback (closed-loop) for precise positioning.
  • Non-Servo Robots — Use mechanical stops (open-loop); limited flexibility.

D. Based on Intelligence Level

  • Manually Controlled — Operated by human in real-time (tele-operated).
  • Fixed Sequence — Performs a pre-set sequence of operations.
  • Variable Sequence — Sequence can be modified easily.
  • Intelligent Robots — Use AI, sensors and vision systems to make decisions.

Workspace (Work Envelope)

Definition Workspace is the total volume of space that can be reached by the robot's end effector (tool tip). It is also called the work envelope or work volume.

The shape and size of the workspace depends on:

  • Robot configuration (Cartesian, Cylindrical, Spherical, Articulated)
  • Number and type of joints
  • Length of links
  • Joint limits (mechanical stops)

Different robot configurations produce different workspace shapes:

ConfigurationWorkspace Shape
Cartesian (PPP)Rectangular prism / box
Cylindrical (RPP)Hollow cylinder
Spherical (RRP)Partial sphere (truncated)
Articulated (RRR)Complex, near-spherical envelope

Fig 1.2 — Workspace shapes for various robot configurations

Degrees of Freedom (DOF)

Definition Degrees of Freedom (DOF) is the number of independent parameters (displacements or rotations) needed to completely define the position and orientation of a body in space.

Each revolute joint (rotation) or prismatic joint (linear slide) contributes 1 DOF. To fully position and orient an object in 3D space, 6 DOF are needed — 3 for position (x, y, z) and 3 for orientation (Roll, Pitch, Yaw).

DOF Examples Human Arm: 7 DOF (3 shoulder + 1 elbow + 3 wrist) PUMA 560 Robot: 6 DOF (all revolute joints) SCARA Robot: 4 DOF (RRP + Rotation) Cartesian: 3 DOF (PPP — 3 prismatic only)

Linear Joint (Type L)

Definition A Linear Joint produces a sliding (translational) motion between the input link and output link. The axes of the two adjacent links are parallel to each other.
  • Type: Prismatic (P) joint
  • Motion: Translational — one link slides linearly relative to the other
  • Joint Variable: Distance d (linear displacement)
  • Axes Relationship: Input and output link axes are parallel
  • Example: The vertical column slide in a Cartesian robot, telescopic mechanism

Fig 1.4 — Linear Joint — translational motion with parallel axes

Twisting Joint (Type T)

Definition A Twisting Joint produces a rotary motion between the input and output links. The axis of rotation is parallel to the axes of both links.
  • Type: Revolute (R) joint
  • Motion: Rotational — one link twists (rotates) about the axis parallel to both links
  • Joint Variable: Angle θ (angular displacement)
  • Axes Relationship: Rotation axis is parallel to both input and output link axes
  • Example: Rotation of the robot forearm about its own longitudinal axis (like turning a screwdriver)

Orthogonal Joint (Type O)

Definition An Orthogonal Joint produces a translational (sliding) motion between the input link and output link where the axes of the two adjacent links are perpendicular (at 90°) to each other.
  • Type: Prismatic (P) joint
  • Motion: Translational — one link slides relative to the other
  • Joint Variable: Distance d
  • Axes Relationship: Input and output link axes are perpendicular
  • Example: Horizontal arm sliding on a vertical column in a cylindrical robot

Fig 1.6 — Orthogonal Joint — translational motion with perpendicular axes

Rotational Joint (Type R)

Definition A Rotational Joint produces a rotary motion where the axis of rotation is perpendicular to the axes of both input and output links.
  • Type: Revolute (R) joint
  • Motion: Rotational — output link rotates about an axis ⊥ to both links
  • Joint Variable: Angle θ
  • Axes Relationship: Rotation axis is perpendicular to axes of both adjacent links
  • Example: Elbow joint of an articulated robot, shoulder joint

Key Observations

  • The human arm has 7 DOF, while most industrial robots have 6 DOF — sufficient for full spatial manipulation.
  • Unlike humans, robots can have superhuman strength (hydraulic actuators), perfect repeatability, and can work in hazardous environments.
  • However, robots lack human adaptability, general intelligence, and fine motor skills for delicate tasks.

Question 06 5 Marks

What are the major components of a robot? Describe Mechanical Subsystems with a neat figure.

Major Components of a Robot

A robot system consists of the following major components:

  1. Mechanical Subsystem — Physical structure (links, joints, gears, end effector)
  2. Electrical/Electronic Subsystem — Power supply, actuators, sensors, wiring
  3. Control Subsystem — Microcontroller / computer, control algorithms
  4. Software/Programming — Robot programming language, path planning, AI
  5. Pneumatic/Hydraulic Subsystem — Fluid power systems (if applicable)

Mechanical Subsystem — Detailed Description

The mechanical subsystem forms the physical skeleton of the robot. It includes:

1. Links (Rigid Bodies)

Links are the rigid structural members that connect joints. They define the robot's reach and form its skeletal frame. Made of aluminium, steel, or carbon fibre.

2. Joints

Joints connect two links and provide relative motion. Types: Linear (L), Orthogonal (O), Rotational (R), Twisting (T), Revolving (V).

3. Base

The fixed or mobile platform on which the robot arm is mounted. Provides stability and support for the entire structure.

4. Wrist

Located between the arm and end effector. Provides orientation control through Roll, Pitch, and Yaw motions.

5. End Effector

The tool or gripper attached to the wrist. Performs the actual task — gripping, welding, painting, drilling, etc.

6. Transmission Elements

Gears, belts, chains, lead screws, harmonic drives — used to transmit motion from actuators to joints.

Question 07 5 Marks

Explain Open-loop and Closed-loop Control Systems in robotic manipulators with suitable block diagrams.

Open-Loop Control System

Definition An open-loop control system is one in which the output has no effect on the input (control action). There is no feedback from the output to the input. The controller sends commands to the actuator without verifying the actual output.

Characteristics:

  • Simple and economical design
  • No feedback mechanism — output is not measured or compared
  • Less accurate — cannot correct for disturbances or errors
  • Generally stable (no chance of oscillation due to feedback)
  • Suitable for simple, predictable tasks

Robotics Example: A stepper motor rotating a fixed number of steps without an encoder to verify the actual angle reached. If the motor misses a step, the system has no way to detect or correct the error.

Fig 1.10 — Open-loop control system — no feedback path

Closed-Loop Control System

Definition A closed-loop control system (feedback control system) is one in which the output is continuously measured and fed back to the input for comparison. The difference (error signal) is used to adjust the control action.

Characteristics:

  • Feedback mechanism present — output is measured by sensors
  • Highly accurate — error is continuously corrected
  • Complex design, higher cost
  • May become unstable if not properly designed
  • Essential for precision robotics tasks

Robotics Example: A servo motor with an encoder. The controller commands a desired angle; the encoder measures the actual angle; the difference (error) drives the motor until the error is zero.

As described in Q6, the major components of a robot include Mechanical, Electrical/Electronic, Control, Software, and Pneumatic/Hydraulic subsystems.

Electrical Subsystem — Detailed Description

The electrical subsystem provides power and signal processing capabilities to the robot.

1. Power Supply

  • Converts AC mains to regulated DC voltages needed by motors, sensors, and controllers.
  • Includes transformers, rectifiers, voltage regulators, and battery packs (for mobile robots).

2. Electric Actuators (Motors)

  • DC Motors — Provide continuous rotation; speed controlled by voltage.
  • Stepper Motors — Rotate in fixed angular steps; open-loop positioning.
  • Servo Motors — DC motor + encoder + controller; precise angle control.
  • BLDC Motors — Brushless DC; high efficiency, long life.

3. Sensors

  • Encoders — Measure angular position/speed of joints.
  • Potentiometers — Analog position sensing.
  • Force/Torque sensors — Measure forces at the end effector.
  • Proximity sensors — Detect nearby objects without contact.

4. Controller / Microprocessor

The "brain" of the robot — receives sensor data, processes commands, sends signals to actuators. Examples: ATmega328P, ARM Cortex, PLC.

5. Wiring and Communication

Cables, connectors, communication buses (I2C, SPI, UART, CAN) that transmit signals and power between components.

What are the major components of a robot? Describe Pneumatic Subsystems with a neat figure.

The major components of a robot are: Mechanical, Electrical/Electronic, Control, Software, and Pneumatic/Hydraulic subsystems (refer Q6).

Pneumatic Subsystem — Detailed Description

Pneumatic systems use compressed air to create linear or rotary motion. They are widely used in robots requiring fast, repetitive, pick-and-place operations.

Components of Pneumatic Subsystem

  1. Air Compressor — Compresses atmospheric air to high pressure (typically 6–10 bar).
  2. Air Filter / Regulator / Lubricator (FRL Unit) — Filters contaminants, regulates pressure, adds lubrication for valve operation.
  3. Direction Control Valve (DCV) — Controls direction of air flow (3/2, 5/2 valves). Can be solenoid-operated.
  4. Flow Control Valve — Controls speed of actuator by regulating air flow rate.
  5. Pneumatic Actuator — Converts compressed air energy into mechanical motion:
    • Linear actuator (cylinder) — Single-acting or double-acting piston-cylinder
    • Rotary actuator — Vane-type pneumatic motor
  6. Exhaust / Muffler — Releases spent air, reduces noise.

Advantages

  • Fast operation and high speed
  • Clean — uses air (no contamination risk)
  • Works in extreme temperatures
  • Simple design and low cost
  • Safe — no risk of fire or explosion

Disadvantages

  • Low force output compared to hydraulic
  • Air is compressible — less precise positioning
  • Air leakage possible at joints and seals
  • Noisy operation
  • Moisture contamination in air supply

Question 10 5 Marks

Elaborate the terms Accuracy, Precision and Repeatability.

1. Accuracy

Definition Accuracy is the ability of the robot to move its end effector to the exact target point in the workspace. It measures how close the actual position is to the programmed (desired) position.
  • Accuracy is a single-event property — measured for one attempt.
  • Expressed as the distance between the desired point and the actual point reached.
  • Depends on: controller resolution, mechanical tolerances, calibration, sensor quality.
  • Analogy: An archer hitting the bullseye — the shot lands exactly where aimed.

2. Precision

Definition Precision is the ability of the robot to reach the same point consistently over multiple attempts. It measures how close multiple attempts are to each other (not necessarily to the target).
  • Precision is a set-of-events property — measured over many repetitions.
  • High precision means the robot lands in the same spot every time, even if that spot is not the target.
  • Analogy: An archer whose shots are all clustered tightly together — consistent but maybe off-center.

3. Repeatability

Definition Repeatability is the ability of the robot to return to a previously taught position with the same accuracy and precision, over and over again. It combines both accuracy and precision.
  • Measured as the spread of positions when the robot is commanded to the same point multiple times.
  • Industrial robots typically have repeatability of ±0.02 mm to ±0.1 mm.
  • More important than accuracy in industrial applications because robots are usually "taught" positions.

Question 11 5 Marks

What is Forward Kinematics? Discuss its importance.

Definition

Definition Forward Kinematics (FK) is the process of determining the position and orientation of the robot's end effector given the joint parameters (angles for revolute joints or displacements for prismatic joints) and the link lengths.

Given: Joint variables (θ₁, θ₂, ..., θₙ) and link lengths (l₁, l₂, ..., lₙ)
Find: End effector position (x, y, z) and orientation (Roll, Pitch, Yaw)

Mathematical Formulation

For a simple 2-link planar robot arm:

Forward Kinematics — 2-Link Planar Arm x = l₁ · cos(θ₁) + l₂ · cos(θ₁ + θ₂) y = l₁ · sin(θ₁) + l₂ · sin(θ₁ + θ₂) Where: l₁, l₂ = link lengths θ₁, θ₂ = joint angles (x, y) = end effector position

Fig 1.15 — Forward Kinematics of a 2-link planar robot arm

Importance of Forward Kinematics

  1. Simulation & Visualization: FK is used in software to simulate robot motion before actual deployment — checking reachability, collisions.
  2. Path Planning: Determines the trajectory of the end effector in Cartesian space from joint-space trajectories.
  3. Workspace Determination: By sweeping all joint variables through their limits, FK helps define the robot's workspace envelope.
  4. Sensor Calibration: FK is used to map sensor readings (joint encoders) to actual end-effector positions.
  5. Foundation for Inverse Kinematics: FK is the mathematical foundation upon which Inverse Kinematics is built — IK requires FK equations to solve for joint angles.
Forward KinematicsInverse Kinematics
Given: Joint angles → Find: End-effector positionGiven: End-effector position → Find: Joint angles

Question 12 5 Marks

Explain the applications of robots in areas that are unsafe or unreachable for humans.

One of the most compelling reasons for using robots is their ability to operate in environments that are too dangerous, inaccessible, or inhospitable for human workers. Key application areas include:

1. Nuclear Industry

  • Robots are used for handling radioactive materials, decommissioning nuclear plants, and inspection of reactor cores.
  • Radiation levels are lethal to humans but do not affect mechanical/electronic systems (with shielding).
  • Example: PackBot used in Fukushima nuclear disaster for reconnaissance.

2. Bomb Disposal / Explosive Ordnance Disposal (EOD)

  • Remote-controlled robots can approach, inspect, and defuse explosive devices.
  • Keeps human bomb disposal technicians at a safe distance.
  • Example: iRobot PackBot 510, Remotec Andros.

3. Space Exploration

  • Planetary rovers explore surfaces of other planets where human survival is impossible.
  • Robotic arms on space stations perform maintenance tasks in the vacuum of space.
  • Examples: Mars rovers (Curiosity, Perseverance), Canadarm2 on ISS.

4. Deep Sea / Underwater Exploration

  • ROVs (Remotely Operated Vehicles) can dive to extreme depths where water pressure would crush humans.
  • Used for ocean floor mapping, underwater pipeline inspection, deep-sea mining.
  • Example: ROV Jason for deep-sea scientific exploration (up to 6,500m depth).

5. Disaster Response & Search and Rescue

  • Robots can enter collapsed buildings, navigate rubble, and detect survivors using thermal/acoustic sensors.
  • Used after earthquakes, mine collapses, and building fires.
  • Example: Snake robots for navigating narrow spaces in rubble.

6. Hazardous Chemical / Biological Environments

  • Chemical plants, biohazard zones, toxic waste handling — robots can operate without risk of poisoning.
  • Used for cleanup of chemical spills and handling of infectious materials.

7. Mining and Tunneling

  • Underground mining is extremely dangerous (cave-ins, toxic gases, explosions).
  • Autonomous mining robots can extract minerals, drill tunnels, and transport ore.

8. Military and Surveillance

  • Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs/drones) for surveillance, reconnaissance.
  • Unmanned Ground Vehicles (UGVs) for mine clearance and patrol.
  • Keeps soldiers out of harm's way.
